## What you need to know

- A promotion that is given for retention rather than performance devalues every promotion that comes after it.
- Level profiles are the foundation of a promotion process that holds. Without them, every promotion decision is subjective and every outcome is contestable.
- The engineers who are most aware of title inflation are your senior engineers. They watch the levels above and below them carefully and notice when the bar shifts.
- A title conversation and a compensation conversation are separate things. Conflating them creates confusion about what each actually means.
- Retroactive correction of title inflation is expensive and disruptive. Prevention is significantly cheaper.

## The core argument

The typical title inflation cycle starts with a retention emergency. A strong engineer receives an outside offer. The founder or manager, unwilling to lose them, offers a promotion as part of the counter. The engineer accepts. Three months later, the engineer who was doing the same work before the outside offer is now a senior engineer where they were previously a mid-level engineer, and nothing about their scope or impact has changed. Every other engineer on the team notices. The next performance review cycle generates three requests for equivalent promotions.

The structural fix is to separate the title decision from the retention decision. If you need to keep an engineer, increase their compensation to market. If they have earned a level change, promote them on the promotion timeline, not the resignation timeline. These are two different decisions that require two different inputs and produce two different signals to the rest of the team. Conflating them produces the inflation cycle.

The process that prevents inflation is a written level profile for each engineering level, used consistently in every performance review. The profile describes the work, not the experience. It says what problems an engineer at this level solves independently, what decisions they make without escalation, and what the visible impact of their work is on the team and the product. An engineer who is performing consistently at the next level's profile gets promoted. One who is performing at the current level gets feedback on what the next level requires. The process is transparent, the standard is consistent, and the title means the same thing before and after the review cycle.

## Common mistakes

1. **Promoting to prevent attrition without a performance basis.** Every time a promotion is used as a retention tool without a genuine level change, the floor of that level drops. This compounds across every subsequent cycle.

2. **Not writing level profiles before the first promotion decision.** The time to write the profiles is before anyone asks for a promotion, not in response to the request. A profile written under pressure to justify an existing decision is a post-hoc rationalization, not a standard.

3. **Using the same title across very different capability levels.** If your senior engineers span from mid-level to near-staff in actual capability, the title has already lost precision. Introducing a structured level between them fixes the problem prospectively.

4. **Not giving clear developmental feedback before a declined promotion.** An engineer who is told they are not ready without being told what ready looks like will either leave or stop developing. Specific, behavioral, forward-looking feedback is the product of a declined promotion conversation.

5. **Allowing the title ladder to diverge across teams.** When different teams use the same titles differently, inter-team comparison creates resentment. Standardize the profiles across the engineering organization early.

## Where to start

1. **Write a one-page level profile for each engineering level.** Three to five bullet points describing the scope of work, the autonomy expected, and the impact demonstrated. Do this before the next performance review cycle begins.

2. **Audit the current title distribution against your intended profiles.** Are there engineers whose current performance is materially below the expectations of their title? That gap is the inflation that needs to be addressed before the next promotion cycle.

3. **Establish the communication practice of citing the level profile in every promotion and declined promotion conversation.** This creates consistency and removes the subjectivity that makes promotion decisions feel arbitrary.
