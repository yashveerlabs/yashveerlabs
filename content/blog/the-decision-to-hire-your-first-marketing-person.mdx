## What you actually need to know

- Hire marketing after you have proven an acquisition channel, not to find one.
- The first hire should own one channel completely, not spread across many.
- Generalists who can write and analyze data outperform specialists and senior leaders at this stage.
- Set a 90-day metric before the hire starts. If the metric does not move, diagnose quickly.
- Founders who hand off marketing before they understand it create dependency on the hire without accountability.

| Hire Profile | Best For | Wrong When |
|---|---|---|
| Content and SEO specialist | Organic channel proven, needs more output | No content strategy exists yet |
| Demand generation specialist | Paid channel proven, needs scaling | Budget is under $5,000/month for ads |
| Growth generalist | Multiple channels to test, need speed | Deep expertise in one channel needed |
| VP of Marketing | Series A, proven channel, need team building | Pre-product-market fit |

## The core argument

Founders hire marketing people when growth is slow. This is the wrong trigger. Slow growth is usually a product problem, a positioning problem, or a sales motion problem, not a marketing capacity problem. A marketing hire does not fix any of these. A marketing hire scales what is already working.

The pattern that ends in a failed marketing hire: the founder has tried several acquisition approaches with inconsistent results. Nothing has really clicked. Growth is below target. The founder hires a marketing person, gives them a budget and a target, and expects them to find the answer. The marketing hire spends three months building a strategy, a quarter trying several things, and delivers results that are similar to what the founder was getting before. The hire was not bad. The problem was not a marketing capacity problem.

The pattern that ends in a successful marketing hire: the founder has been writing content for six months and traffic is growing. The bottleneck is output volume, not strategy. The hire knows what to do because the founder has already figured it out. Their job is to do more of it faster, not to figure out what to do.

The decision to hire is really a question: have I found the channel that works for my product and my ICP? If yes, hire to scale it. If no, keep experimenting yourself.

## What to hand off and what to keep

The transition from founder-led marketing to hired marketing is not a clean handoff. The founder retains strategic decision-making even after the hire. What changes is the execution capacity.

The founder owns: the positioning, the ICP definition, the value narrative, the decision about which channels to prioritize, and the evaluation of what is working. These decisions require product knowledge and customer context that a new hire does not have.

The hire owns: execution within the chosen channel. Content production on the editorial calendar. Campaign management within the paid strategy. Community management within the community channel. Speed and volume within a defined direction.

The failure mode: the founder hands off everything including strategy and then evaluates the hire on revenue growth. Marketing strategy decisions made without deep product knowledge produce weak content and poorly targeted campaigns. The hire makes decisions they do not have the context for, and the results reflect that.

## Practical skills to screen for

The first marketing hire needs to be able to execute without direction on the tactical level. This means:

**Writing.** Can they produce a 1,500-word post that is clear, specific, and useful to the target reader? Ask for a sample on a technical topic relevant to the product. Vague, generic writing is easy to produce. Specific, useful writing for a technical audience is the skill that matters.

**Analytics.** Can they look at Google Search Console, a paid campaign dashboard, or a conversion funnel and identify what is and is not working? Ask for an example of a time they changed a campaign or content strategy based on data and what happened.

**Speed.** Can they publish something, see that it is not working, and change course without a six-week process? The early-stage marketing environment rewards speed. Slow execution in a fast-changing channel is a liability.

## Common mistakes founders make with first marketing hires

1. Hiring a VP of Marketing who manages rather than executes. At early stage, the marketing team is one person. A manager of zero people is a strategist with no one to execute. You need execution, not strategy.
2. Not giving the hire access to customer feedback. Marketing that is not grounded in customer language produces content that does not resonate. The hire needs to talk to customers.
3. Hiring based on experience at a large company. Marketing at a 500-person company is fundamentally different from marketing at a 10-person company. Large company experience does not transfer directly.
4. Not setting a 90-day metric before the hire starts. Without a defined success metric, the evaluation is subjective and usually unfair to both sides.
5. Allowing the hire to change the channel strategy in the first 90 days. If the founder has proven that content works and the hire wants to run paid ads, that is a strategy conversation, not a permission the hire has unilaterally.

## Where to start: a 3-step marketing hire process

**Step 1: Document what you have already figured out.** What channel has produced your best customers? What content or campaigns have worked? What does the ideal customer look like? Write these down before writing the job description. They define the scope.

**Step 2: Write a 90-day plan for the hire before interviewing.** What metric will they own? What output level is expected? What resources are available? This plan is the hiring filter. Share it with candidates and ask them to respond to it. Their response tells you whether they understand the scope.

**Step 3: Hire for execution speed and content quality, not for title or brand experience.** Run a practical assignment: write a LinkedIn post on a topic relevant to our ICP, or analyze this campaign data and tell me what you would change. The assignment reveals execution ability that interviews cannot.
