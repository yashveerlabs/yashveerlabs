## What you actually need to know

- The Director of Engineering hire is justified by a coordination problem: the senior engineering leader has more managers than they can effectively support.
- This hire is high-risk because a Director who is not operating at scope creates overhead without adding value. Qualify candidates rigorously for their ability to manage managers, not just engineers.
- Promoting from within is lower risk than external hiring when an internal candidate has the capability. Internal candidates have context; external candidates have the resume.
- The Director role fails most often when the scope is unclear -- the Director does not know which decisions they own and which require escalation.
- The first 90 days should be spent building relationships with the engineering managers who report to them, not making structural changes.

| Signal | Director Hire Is Timely | Director Hire Is Premature |
|---|---|---|
| Engineering manager count | 4+ reporting to senior leader | 2-3, manageable directly |
| Engineering team size | 30-60 engineers | Under 30 engineers |
| Senior leader's time | >50% on engineering coordination | Balanced across functions |
| Decision bottlenecks | Coordination decisions waiting | Decisions moving efficiently |

## The core argument

The Director of Engineering is a coordination hire. The engineering team has grown to the point where the senior engineering leader -- the VP of Engineering or CTO -- has more engineering managers reporting to them than they can provide meaningful support to. The Director takes responsibility for a subset of those managers and their teams, allowing the senior leader to reduce their span of control to a manageable size.

This is the condition that justifies the Director hire. It is a narrow condition, which is why premature Director hires are so common. A startup with two engineering managers and 15 engineers does not need a Director of Engineering -- the VP can manage two engineering managers. A startup with six engineering managers and 45 engineers has a legitimate coordination problem that a Director can help solve.

The Director hire that is made for other reasons -- to give a high-performing engineer a career path, to have a more impressive org chart for recruiting purposes, to create an internal check on the technical decision-making -- is almost always premature. The role requires a specific problem to solve (the span of control problem) and the authority to solve it (the ability to make decisions about the teams they manage). Without the problem and the authority, the Director is an expensive layer with nothing to do.

The most common failure mode: the Director is hired, given an impressive title, and placed in an organization where all the real decisions are still being made by the VP or the CTO. The Director has no real authority and no specific problem to solve. They become an escalation point for their managers (who continue to make decisions at the VP level anyway) and a source of confusion for the team about who actually owns what.

## What the Director role actually involves

The Director of Engineering manages engineering managers. This is categorically different from managing engineers. The skill set required for each is different, and the experience that predicts success in each role is different.

A Director who manages engineering managers well: provides feedback to managers on how they are developing their engineers, helps managers navigate difficult personnel decisions, identifies and resolves coordination issues between the teams managed, sets technical and process standards for the engineering organizations they manage, and represents the engineering perspective in cross-functional planning discussions.

This requires experience managing managers -- having given feedback to a manager about how they are managing their team, having coached a manager through a personnel decision, having navigated cross-team dependencies at the level above individual contributor. A strong individual contributor and a strong engineering manager do not automatically make a strong Director. The additional experience at the "managing managers" level is required.

## Qualifying candidates

The interview process for the first Director of Engineering should be more rigorous than the interview process for an engineering manager. The role has more leverage -- a good Director improves the entire engineering organization they manage, and a bad Director degrades it.

The specific questions to probe: describe a time when you had to give critical feedback to an engineering manager about how they were managing their team. What did you observe, what did you say, and what changed? Describe a cross-team dependency that was blocking progress in a team you managed. How did you resolve it? What is your framework for deciding which decisions to make yourself and which to escalate to your supervisor?

These questions require specific experience at the Director level. Candidates who have only managed individual contributors will give answers about managing engineers, not about managing managers. The distinction is significant and reveals whether the candidate is ready to operate at scope.

## The onboarding that determines whether the hire works

The first 90 days for a new Director of Engineering should be focused on context and relationship building, not on making changes. The Director who arrives and immediately restructures teams, changes the technical standards, or replaces managers is making changes without the context required to make them well.

A better first 90 days: individual conversations with each engineering manager to understand what is working and what is frustrating in their current situation. Review of the technical roadmap and the in-flight projects to understand the technical context. Review of recent incidents and postmortems to understand the reliability situation. A synthesis of these inputs, shared with the VP of Engineering, that identifies the highest-leverage areas for improvement.

The changes that result from this synthesis are more likely to be correct than the changes that are made before it. The Director who invests in context first and action second earns the trust of the engineering managers faster and makes better decisions with the authority they have been given.

## Common mistakes companies make with the first Director of Engineering

1. Hiring the Director before the span of control problem is real. A Director of Engineering managing two engineering managers is an expensive solution to a problem that does not exist yet.
2. Not defining the Director's decision authority clearly. The Director who does not know which decisions they own will either over-escalate (creating bottlenecks) or over-decide (creating friction with the VP). A clear decision authority matrix -- which decisions the Director owns, which require VP involvement -- is required.
3. Promoting the best engineer to Director without confirming manager-of-managers capability. The strongest technical individual contributor is not automatically the right choice for a Director role. The required skill set is different.
4. Making the Director responsible for technical excellence without giving them authority over technical standards. A Director who is held accountable for engineering quality but who cannot enforce code review standards, test coverage requirements, or deployment processes has accountability without authority.
5. Not providing coaching for the first Director. This is a new role with new challenges. The VP of Engineering who does not provide ongoing coaching and feedback to the new Director will find the Director struggling with situations they have not encountered before.

## Where to start: a 3-step Director evaluation

**Step 1: Assess the span of control problem.** How many engineering managers currently report to the senior engineering leader? What is the quality of the feedback and development each manager is receiving? If the answer to the second question is "insufficient," the span of control problem may be real.

**Step 2: Evaluate internal candidates before looking externally.** Which engineering managers have demonstrated the ability to manage other people's work effectively? Which have given feedback that changed another manager's approach? Which have resolved cross-team dependencies without escalating to senior leadership? These are the indicators of Director-level capability.

**Step 3: Define the role clearly before posting the job description.** What decisions will the Director own? What problems are they being hired to solve? How will success be measured at 90 days, 6 months, and 12 months? These answers should exist before the first candidate interview, not after the first candidate is hired.
