## What you actually need to know

- The service business is a real business. Treat it that way.
- First clients come from network. Next clients come from visible work.
- Project pricing beats hourly pricing.
- Day rates for senior engineers in 2026 are 1000 to 3000 USD.
- The sales and admin work is the price of running the business.

| Service type | Typical engagement | Pricing |
|---|---|---|
| Code review for funded startups | 3 to 5 days | 5k to 15k USD |
| Architecture consultation | 1 to 2 weeks | 10k to 30k USD |
| Migration leadership | 2 to 6 months | 50k to 200k USD |
| Production incident response | Retainer or per incident | 3k to 10k USD per incident |
| Performance audit | 2 to 3 weeks | 15k to 40k USD |
| Security review | 1 to 2 weeks | 10k to 30k USD |

## The core argument

A senior engineer has a profitable service business sitting inside their skill set. They do not realize it because nobody framed it that way. The skill is the depth they have built. The market is the supply of problems that match the depth. The price is set by the value, not by the hours.

The barrier is not technical. The barrier is the discomfort of asking for money. Most senior engineers will help a friend for free and quietly resent the time. The reframe is to ask for fair compensation and deliver clearly scoped work. The friendship survives. The engineer gets paid. The client gets a better outcome because the engagement is professional.

The math is favorable. A senior engineer charging 1500 USD per day who books fifty days per year is generating 75k USD of additional income. The work is on top of the full time job, not instead of it. The cumulative effect over five years is significant. The career optionality it creates is more significant.

The trap is treating it as a side hustle. The hustle framing keeps it low priority and low quality. The business framing forces the engineer to take it seriously. Real contracts, real invoices, real deadlines. The clients respond to the seriousness. The price reflects the value.

## How to set up the practice

| Asset | Investment |
|---|---|
| Personal website with services page | One weekend |
| Sample contract template | A few hours with a lawyer |
| Invoicing tool | Hours, then minutes per invoice |
| Accounting setup | Hours to set up, ongoing minimal |
| Insurance | A few hundred dollars per year |
| Incorporation | A few hundred to a few thousand depending on jurisdiction |
| Pipeline tracking | Notion or a simple CRM |

The total setup time is a few weekends. The cost is under 2000 USD in most jurisdictions. The payback comes from the first client.

## How to find the first clients

The first three come from people who already know you. Tell former colleagues you are taking specific kinds of work. Tell the founders you have worked with. Tell the engineers who have asked your opinion. Be specific about the type of engagement you want. Vague availability produces vague leads.

The next ten come from visible work. Blog posts that show the depth you have. Conference talks that present your thinking. GitHub projects that demonstrate the skill. The work is the marketing. The right clients find you because they read what you wrote.

The trap is trying to scale before the practice is repeatable. The first ten clients teach you what your offering should be. The eleventh client should fit a clear pattern. If every client is unique, the practice is not yet a business.

## How much does this cost

The financial cost is small. A few thousand dollars for setup. A few hundred per month for tools. The hidden cost is time. Sales conversations, scoping calls, contract negotiation, invoice follow ups. Each is small. Together they add up to roughly twenty percent of the engagement time. That overhead is built into the pricing or it eats the margin.

The other hidden cost is context switching. Doing client work in the evenings is different from doing client work between 9 and 5. The engineer has to manage their own energy. The ones who succeed pick predictable blocks of time and protect them.

## Features the practice must have

- A clear list of services with pricing.
- A standard contract template.
- An invoicing system that runs without effort.
- A pipeline tracker for leads.
- Insurance appropriate to the jurisdiction.
- An accountant on retainer once revenue starts.
- A way to say no without burning the relationship.

## How this is playing out for me

Yashveer Labs is the practice I am building. Five production systems on the board. The work has been a mix of full builds and consultations. The pricing has moved from hourly in year zero to project based in year one. The clients come through the work, through the network, and now increasingly through inbound from the writing.

The pieces I underestimated were the sales and admin work. The first contract took longer than the first project. The second contract took an hour. The pattern compounds. The lessons from each engagement go into the template for the next.

The expectation for the next eighteen months is that the practice grows past the time I can give it alone. At that point the decision is to scale by adding contractors or to stay focused on the work I do personally. Both are valid. The right answer depends on what kind of business I want to run.

## Common mistakes engineers make

1. Treating the business as a side hustle.
2. Pricing by the hour. Rewards inefficiency.
3. Offering everything. Confuses the market.
4. Hiding outside work from a current employer. Goes badly.
5. No contract. The scope drifts. The relationship sours.
6. No insurance. One bad outcome erases the gains.
7. Doing too much without an accountant. Tax season punishes.
8. No way to say no. Burnout follows.

## A 90 day plan

1. **Month one.** Set up the website, the services page, the contract template, the invoicing tool. Tell three former colleagues.
2. **Month two.** Land the first engagement. Deliver well. Document the lessons.
3. **Month three.** Land the second engagement. Refine the offering. Update the pricing.
