# The CTO vs VP Engineering Distinction

**The CTO and the VP of Engineering are two different jobs that are often treated as one, resulting in the wrong person in the wrong role and a technical leadership structure that serves neither function well. The CTO is an externally-facing technical visionary who sets the long-term direction and represents the technical strategy to investors, partners, and customers. The VP of Engineering is an internally-facing execution leader who manages the engineering team, the delivery process, and the organizational structure. Both are critical. Neither is interchangeable.**

## What you need to know

- The CTO role is fundamentally strategic and external: it defines the technical vision and represents the company's technology to the outside world
- The VP of Engineering role is fundamentally operational and internal: it manages the engineering team and the delivery pipeline
- A strong engineer who cannot manage people should be the CTO, not the VP of Engineering
- A strong engineering manager who cannot set technical vision should be the VP of Engineering, not the CTO
- Most early-stage startups cannot afford both roles and should consciously decide which function to prioritize given the company's current needs

## The core argument

The confusion between these two roles comes from the fact that in a three-person startup, one technical co-founder is doing both jobs. They are setting the technical vision because no one else is, and they are managing the engineering work because there is no one else to do it. As the company grows and the first engineering hires appear, the question of who does what becomes necessary, and many founders resolve it by promoting the strongest engineer rather than thinking about which function the company most needs to formalize.

The CTO's job at a growth-stage company is to make decisions about the technical direction that will be correct two years from now. Which architectural patterns will scale? Which technology bets should the company make? How should the technical roadmap connect to the business strategy? These are questions that require deep technical judgment combined with business acumen, but they do not require the ability to run a 30-person engineering organization. A CTO who is also managing sprint planning, conducting performance reviews, and resolving team conflicts is not doing the CTO job. They are doing the VP of Engineering job.

The VP of Engineering's job is to make the engineering team productive and the delivery process reliable. They care about hiring pipelines, engineering culture, on-call rotations, incident response processes, and whether the team is shipping what was promised on the timeline that was committed. They do not need to be the deepest technical thinker in the room. They need to be the person who can grow a team from five engineers to fifty without the team breaking down. These are management skills, not engineering skills, and treating them as the same has cost many startups both a good engineer and a good manager at once.

## Common mistakes

1. **Promoting the best engineer to VP of Engineering.** Engineering excellence and engineering management excellence are different skills. The best engineer in a company is often not the best choice for a management role and may perform poorly in it while also losing the high-leverage individual contributor work they were excellent at.
2. **Treating the CTO as a glorified senior engineer.** A CTO who is spending most of their time writing code is not doing the strategic and representational work that justifies the CTO title. This is usually a sign that the company does not have enough technical staff and is pulling the CTO into individual contributor work.
3. **Hiring a VP of Engineering before the engineering team exists.** A VP of Engineering needs an engineering team to manage. Hiring this role before there are at least five to eight engineers creates a manager with no one to manage, which usually results in the VP Engineering doing individual contributor work and the role becoming redundant.
4. **Conflating technical authority with management authority.** In some engineering cultures, the person with the most technical authority is expected to also have management authority. These can and should be separated. The CTO can have technical authority without being a manager. The VP of Engineering can have management authority without being the deepest technical mind.
5. **Not defining the reporting structure clearly.** If both the CTO and the VP of Engineering exist in the organization without a clear delineation of who makes which decisions, the engineering team will receive conflicting direction. Define which role owns which decisions before hiring for both.

## Where to start

**Step 1: Write a job description for each role as it applies to your company.** The exercise of writing the description forces clarity about what you actually need. If you find that you are writing the same description for both roles, you have not yet articulated the distinction for your specific context.

**Step 2: Assess your current technical leadership against both descriptions.** Who is currently doing the CTO work? Who is doing the VP of Engineering work? If one person is doing both, which one is getting less attention than the company needs?

**Step 3: Hire for the function you are under-serving, not for the title.** A company that has strong technical vision but poor delivery processes needs VP of Engineering capacity. A company that has strong delivery processes but weak technical strategy needs CTO capacity. The hiring decision should follow the gap.
