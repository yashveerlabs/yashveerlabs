## What you actually need to know

- The conference is worth the money if the speaker list is practitioners, not primarily vendor advocates.
- Speaking at a conference is three to five times more valuable than attending one.
- The hallway track -- conversations between sessions -- produces the highest-quality connections at any conference.
- Online conference content is valuable. Online conference networking is not equivalent to in-person.
- One high-quality conference per year in your specific domain is better than four general programming conferences.

| Conference Type | Networking Value | Learning Value | Career Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Domain-specific (e.g., RailsConf, KubeCon) | Very high: audience shares your context | High: talks are specific | High: becomes known in the domain |
| General programming (e.g., GOTO, NDC) | Medium: broad audience | Medium: useful survey | Medium: broader but less targeted |
| Vendor-organized (e.g., re:Invent, Google Cloud Next) | Medium: vendor relationships | Medium: product updates | Low to medium: vendor context heavy |
| Academic (e.g., PLDI, OSDI) | Low for industry | Very high: cutting edge | High for research roles |
| Local meetups | High: local network | Low to medium | Medium: local community |

## The core argument

Most engineering conferences are not worth traveling for. The speaker selection process selects for people who submitted a proposal, not necessarily for people who have done the most interesting work. The vendor sponsorship model creates incentives to feature speakers who are promoting vendor products. The attendee mix skews toward people who are earlier in their careers and looking to learn rather than senior engineers and decision-makers.

The conferences worth traveling for have solved these problems. They have speaker selection committees that actively recruit practitioners doing interesting work, not just accepting whoever submits. They limit vendor control over the program. They attract an audience that includes senior engineers, engineering leaders, and founders in the domain. When you are in the hallway at one of these conferences, the person next to you is likely working on a problem similar to yours, has the authority to make decisions, and is open to a conversation.

The way to identify these conferences is through practitioners in your domain, not through conference marketing. Ask the senior engineers you respect which conferences they attend. Look at which conferences produce talks that get shared and referenced by practitioners online. Look at the speaker history: are these people who have shipped production systems and written substantive technical content, or are they primarily on the conference circuit?

The conferences I have found useful -- both for learning and for relationship building -- have specific, technical talk tracks, speakers who are clearly practitioners rather than primarily speakers, and attendee demographics that include the people I want to know. The names shift over time as conference quality changes, which is why the practitioner network is a better source than a static list.

## Getting value out of speaking

Speaking at a conference where attendance would otherwise be valuable is one of the most efficient career investments available to an engineer. The return is asymmetric: the time investment is comparable (travel, preparation), but the visibility is orders of magnitude higher. A talk at a major conference reaches thousands of practitioners in your domain. A recording circulates indefinitely. Your name is on the program associated with a credible institution.

Getting accepted to speak at top-tier conferences takes time and multiple submissions. The approach that works: start with local meetups, then regional conferences, then national conferences. Each level builds a track record of delivering useful talks and creates references that support later applications. The talk topics that get accepted at competitive conferences are specific and technical -- not "Introduction to Kubernetes" but "How We Reduced Our Kubernetes Costs by 60% by Implementing Vertical Pod Autoscaling."

When you are accepted, the talk is the minimum viable product. The preparation that matters is: who do you want to meet at this conference, and how can the talk facilitate those introductions? After a talk, the people who found it useful will approach you. These are the highest-quality conversations you will have at the conference because the other person has already self-selected as interested in your work.

## Making the investment worthwhile

Conference travel is expensive. The typical major conference costs $300 to $2,000 for registration, plus travel and accommodation, plus time away from work. To justify this, the conference needs to produce specific outcomes: knowledge that accelerates current work, relationships that lead to opportunities, or visibility that builds reputation.

Planning before the conference determines whether these outcomes are achieved. Before attending, identify: three specific technical questions you want to explore, three people you want to meet (look at the speaker list and attendee list if available), and one output you will produce from the experience (a blog post, an internal summary, an open source contribution inspired by what you learned).

During the conference, prioritize the hallway. Go to sessions strategically -- the ones by speakers you most want to meet, or the ones covering topics directly relevant to current work. For the rest, the hallway conversations, the lunch tables, the evening events are where the highest-value interactions happen. Most people at conferences are there because they are engaged practitioners. They are worth talking to.

After the conference, follow up within a week. The relationships built in a hallway conversation become weak ties very quickly. A brief note with something specific from the conversation -- "I looked into the approach you mentioned for distributed tracing and found this" -- keeps the connection alive.

## Common mistakes engineers make with conferences

1. Going to conferences without a goal. "See what is interesting" produces a week of context-switching and comes home with a bag of sponsor swag and no meaningful outcomes.
2. Spending all the time in sessions. The content is usually available as recordings. The hallway track is not.
3. Not submitting to speak because the proposal might be rejected. Submit anyway. Most proposals are rejected and it does not matter. Acceptance is asymmetrically valuable.
4. Attending the same general conference repeatedly out of habit. If the conference is not producing outcomes, try a different one or the same domain with a smaller, more focused audience.
5. Not following up afterward. A conference connection that is not followed up within a week fades. The investment in the relationship is lost.

## Where to start: a 3-step conference strategy

**Step 1: Identify the two most relevant conferences in your specific technical domain.** Not the most famous general conference. The conference where the practitioners who are most advanced in your specific area gather. Ask practitioners in your network which conferences they attend and find useful.

**Step 2: Submit a talk proposal to both conferences.** For each one, identify a specific, technical topic from your recent work that would be genuinely useful to the audience. Write the proposal as if you are explaining the talk to a senior engineer in your domain. Be specific about what the audience will be able to do differently after seeing the talk.

**Step 3: Attend whichever conference is most accessible this year, speaker or not, with a written goal list.** Before arriving, write down three people you want to meet, three questions you want to explore, and one thing you will produce from the experience. Review the list on the last day and evaluate what was achieved.
