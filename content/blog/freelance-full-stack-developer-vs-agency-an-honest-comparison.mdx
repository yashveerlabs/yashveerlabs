## What you actually need to know

- Freelancers cost 2 to 5 times less per hour than agencies.
- Freelancers work directly with the founder. Agencies mediate.
- Agencies provide multiple disciplines and redundancy.
- The trial sprint is the most reliable evaluation for either.
- The wrong choice costs both money and time.

| Option | Best fit | Hourly rate | Communication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Freelance senior full stack | Clear scope, direct engagement | 100 to 250 USD | Direct |
| Freelance specialist | Specific deep work | 150 to 350 USD | Direct |
| Boutique agency (small, senior) | Multi discipline projects | 200 to 400 USD | Some mediation |
| Mid size agency | Complex projects with project management | 250 to 500 USD | Heavy mediation |
| Large agency | Enterprise scale projects | 400 to 1000 USD | Layered mediation |

## The core argument

The freelancer versus agency choice comes up for almost every non technical founder who needs to build software. The answer depends on the project shape and the founder's appetite for direct engagement. The wrong choice costs both money and the project's success.

A freelancer is the right call when the scope is clear and the founder can engage directly. The freelancer charges less per hour. The founder works directly with the engineer doing the work. The communication is unmediated. The cost is the founder's time and the single point of failure if the freelancer becomes unavailable.

An agency is the right call when the project needs multiple disciplines or when the founder cannot engage directly. The agency provides the engineering, design, project management, and account management. The single point of contact is the account manager who routes work internally. The cost is the markup that covers the agency's overhead.

The mistake teams make is treating the choice as universal. A simple project that fits a freelancer is given to an agency at three times the cost. A complex project that needs multiple disciplines is given to a freelancer who struggles to cover all the work alone. Each is the wrong call.

The evaluation method is the same for both. References from past clients. A trial sprint with the actual engineers who will do the work. Clear scope and acceptance criteria. The trial sprint reveals the actual quality and the actual communication patterns. The trial that goes well usually predicts the full project. The trial that struggles usually predicts trouble.

## The decision map

| Question | Freelancer | Agency |
|---|---|---|
| Is the scope clear? | Yes | Either |
| Need multiple disciplines? | No | Yes |
| Can the founder engage directly? | Yes | Less critical |
| Is redundancy important? | Less | Yes |
| Is budget a constraint? | Yes | Less |
| Need a single point of contact? | Less | Yes |
| Is communication speed important? | Yes | Less |
| Is the project under three months? | Yes | Either |
| Is the project enterprise scale? | Less | Yes |

## Features the engagement must have

- Documented scope.
- Trial sprint before commitment.
- Clear communication cadence.
- Defined deliverables and acceptance criteria.
- IP transfer terms.
- Termination terms.
- Reference checks.
- A clear point of contact and escalation path.

## How this played out on real projects

A non technical founder I advised wanted to hire an agency for an MVP build. The estimate was 320k USD. The project scope was clear and well documented. The founder was available for direct engagement.

We hired a senior freelance full stack developer instead. The total cost was 95k USD. The MVP shipped in five months. The quality was high because the founder worked directly with the engineer. The agency would have produced a similar outcome at over three times the cost.

A different founder hired a freelancer for a project that needed engineering, design, mobile, and project management. The freelancer was excellent but could not cover all the disciplines alone. The project stalled at the design and mobile work. We replaced the freelancer with a boutique agency. The cost was higher but the project shipped.

## Common mistakes founders make

1. Hiring an agency for a project that fits a freelancer.
2. Hiring a freelancer for a project that needs multiple disciplines.
3. No reference checks.
4. No trial sprint.
5. Vague scope. Disputes follow.
6. No clear deliverables.
7. Comparing prices without comparing what is included.
8. Treating brand recognition as a substitute for quality.

## A 30 day evaluation framework

1. **Week one.** Define the project scope. Identify the disciplines needed.
2. **Week two.** Source candidates. Run reference checks.
3. **Week three.** Trial sprint with the top candidate.
4. **Week four.** Decide. Document the engagement.
