## What you actually need to know

- The funnel requires 40-100 applicants per hire at most companies. If the application volume is low, the hire is not possible at the target quality bar.
- The strongest candidates have the highest dropout rate at the technical assessment stage. Assessments that are too long or irrelevant to the actual work cause the most experienced candidates to withdraw.
- Speed is the competitive differentiator in engineering hiring. The company that makes an offer in two weeks wins candidates that the company with a six-week process loses to other offers.
- Offer-to-signed conversion rates below 70 percent indicate a misalignment in compensation or level that should be surfaced in the recruiter screen, not discovered at the offer.
- Every stage of the hiring process is evaluated by the candidate. The candidate who has a disorganized interview experience learns that the team lacks operational discipline before they even start.

## The core argument

Most startup engineering hiring fails not because the evaluation is wrong but because the funnel is slow, the assessment is designed for the wrong candidate, and the process signals disorganization to candidates who are evaluating the company as much as the company is evaluating them.

The engineering candidate who is currently employed and productive has no urgency to move quickly through a hiring process. They will complete a take-home assessment if it is reasonable. They will show up for an interview if it is well-organized. They will accept an offer if it is competitive and arrives before the other offers they are evaluating. If any of these conditions is not met, they withdraw -- not with hostility, but simply because the alternative of staying where they are is available.

This is the competitive reality of engineering hiring: the candidates that companies most want to hire have the most options and the lowest urgency. The companies that hire them most consistently are the ones that have built a process that is fast, well-organized, respectful of the candidate's time, and transparent about the role and compensation early.

## Stage 1: Application review

The application review stage determines which applicants receive a recruiter screen. The most common failure at this stage: reviewing resumes for pedigree (specific company names, specific degree pedigrees) rather than for evidence of relevant capability.

A resume that is not from a FAANG or tier-1 university but shows a portfolio of shipped projects in the relevant technology, clear ownership of features with measurable outcomes, and evidence of technical growth is a better predictor of performance than a pedigree resume with no clear evidence of shipped work.

The structured resume screen uses a scorecard with specific criteria: relevant technical experience (years in the specific technologies the role requires), evidence of ownership (descriptions that indicate they built and owned things, not just contributed to them), communication quality (the resume itself is a writing sample), and specificity of achievements (specific numbers versus vague claims).

Applying this scorecard consistently reduces the bias toward pedigree and increases the signal quality of the review stage.

## Stage 2: The recruiter screen

The recruiter screen is a 30-minute call to verify basic fit before investing significant time in technical evaluation. The recruiter covers: compensation expectations, timeline availability (when the candidate is available to start), the role in enough detail for the candidate to verify their interest, and any logistical constraints (remote vs. in-office requirements, geographic restrictions).

The most valuable information to collect at this stage: compensation expectations. The candidate who expects $200,000 for a role budgeted at $140,000 should discover the misalignment at 30 minutes, not after five hours of technical interviews. Discussing compensation in the recruiter screen prevents the offer rejection that costs everyone time.

The recruiter screen is also the candidate's first experience with the company's people. A recruiter who is well-prepared, knows the role, and can speak to the engineering culture and the team's projects is a competitive advantage. A recruiter who is reading from a script and cannot answer basic questions about the role signals a poorly organized hiring process.

## Stage 3: The technical phone screen

The technical phone screen (30-45 minutes with an engineer) assesses technical foundation: can the candidate discuss technical concepts clearly, do they have the depth required for the role, and do they approach problems with the reasoning that the team needs?

The most common failure at this stage: using the technical phone screen as a continuation of the recruiter screen (too much discussion, too little technical assessment) or as a full technical interview (too much coding, not enough conversation). The phone screen should produce enough signal to make a confident go/no-go decision on the assessment or onsite, not to make the final hiring decision.

A well-designed technical phone screen: 15 minutes of conversation about the candidate's recent work and technical decisions, 20 minutes of technical discussion (system design, code review, or architectural questions appropriate to the role), and 10 minutes for the candidate's questions. This produces a strong signal about technical depth without consuming the candidate's full afternoon.

## Stage 4: The technical assessment

The technical assessment is where strong candidates drop most often. The reasons: the take-home is too long (more than 4 hours for an experienced engineer is above the threshold where many candidates will decline), the problem is a generic algorithm exercise that does not resemble the actual work, or the timeline for completion is too long (candidates who are serious about the opportunity want to complete it quickly and get a response).

The assessment that retains the strongest candidates: realistic (similar to the actual work they will do), bounded (4 hours maximum for a senior engineer, 2 hours for a screen), fast to evaluate (the team should respond within 2 business days of submission), and designed to reveal approach and judgment rather than to test memorized algorithms.

An alternative that works well for experienced engineers: skip the take-home for candidates with strong portfolio evidence and proceed directly to a technical discussion or a pair programming session during the onsite. This respects the candidate's time and produces a different kind of signal (reasoning under observation versus polished prepared work).

## Common mistakes companies make in their hiring funnel

1. Moving too slowly between stages. A candidate who submits an assessment on Monday and hears nothing by Friday has started assuming a rejection and is continuing their other processes.
2. Making the take-home too long. Experienced engineers value their time. An 8-hour take-home filters out the candidates who have other options, which is a filter in the wrong direction.
3. Not briefing interviewers before the loop. A 4-person interview loop where two interviewers ask the same questions covers the same ground twice and leaves other areas uncovered.
4. Not discussing compensation until the offer. Compensation misalignment discovered at offer means everyone wasted their time.
5. Not closing the loop quickly after the final interview. The candidate who finishes their final interview on Friday and hears nothing until the following Wednesday is evaluating other offers in the meantime. Close within 48 hours.

## Where to start: a 3-step funnel audit

**Step 1: Calculate the conversion rate at each stage for your last 10 hires.** Application to screen, screen to assessment, assessment to onsite, onsite to offer, offer to signed. Any stage with a conversion rate significantly below the typical ranges in the table above is worth investigating.

**Step 2: Ask three candidates who declined or withdrew in the last six months why they dropped out.** The feedback from withdrawn candidates is the most honest signal about what is not working in the process. Most companies do not ask this question.

**Step 3: Reduce the total time from first contact to offer to three weeks or less.** Identify the scheduling delays in the current process (the days between stages where the candidate is waiting) and eliminate them. Calendar coordination, assessment turnaround time, and offer preparation are the typical sources of delay.
