## What you actually need to know

- Senior full stack developer for the unshipped product.
- Fractional CTO for architecture, hiring, and process at scale.
- Most early stage SaaS picks the developer first.
- The combination is powerful but expensive.
- A senior developer with CTO judgment is the rare ideal.

| Role | Cost monthly | Best fit |
|---|---|---|
| Senior full stack developer | 12000 to 25000 USD | Building the product |
| Fractional CTO | 5000 to 20000 USD | Architecture and judgment |
| Full time CTO | 25000 to 45000 USD plus equity | Funded scaling teams |
| Senior developer with CTO judgment | 15000 to 30000 USD | Rare ideal |
| Combination of CTO plus developer | 25000 to 45000 USD | Funded teams |

## The core argument

The fractional CTO versus senior full stack developer question comes up often for non technical founders trying to figure out who they need. The answer depends on what is missing. The founder who needs judgment but not hands on hires a fractional CTO. The founder who needs hands on building hires a developer.

Most early stage SaaS needs the developer more than the CTO. The product has not shipped. The team needs hands on time to build. The fractional CTO gives advice but does not build. The advice without execution does not produce product. The product is what matters at this stage.

The fractional CTO adds real value at specific moments. The non technical founder evaluating technical hires. The fundraising conversation that needs technical credibility. The architecture decision that will shape the next two years. Each is a high stakes moment where senior judgment is worth meaningful money per hour.

The combination of fractional CTO plus senior developer is the right answer for funded teams that need both. The CTO sets the technical direction. The developer executes it. The team has the strategic guidance and the shipping capacity together. The cost is real but the leverage is correspondingly large.

The rare ideal is the senior developer with CTO level judgment. The person who can both execute and direct. The cost is higher than a typical senior developer. The savings versus the combination of two roles is real. The trade off is finding the person, who is rare in the market.

## Features the hire must have

- Production experience shipping the kind of product you are building.
- Clear communication that the founder understands.
- A track record of working with non technical founders if applicable.
- References from previous engagements.
- A scope that matches the founder's actual needs.
- A contract that protects both sides.

## Expert opinion

> The fractional CTO market has grown because non technical founders are looking for the senior judgment they lack. The match is good for some situations and bad for others. The honest read is that most early stage SaaS needs hands on building more than strategic judgment. The fractional CTO is the right call when the product is shipping and the architecture is the bottleneck. Before that, the senior developer is the higher leverage hire.
>

## How this played out on real projects

A non technical founder I advised wanted to hire a fractional CTO at the seed stage. The product had not shipped. The team was the founder plus a developer who was not senior. The fractional CTO was going to give advice that the team could not execute.

We reset the hire. The senior full stack developer joined first. The product shipped four months later. The fractional CTO came on at month seven when the architecture decisions for the next year were the bottleneck. The two roles combined effectively because the order matched the actual needs.

A different founder hired a fractional CTO first. The architecture advice was good but unactioned because the team could not execute. The product did not ship for nine months. The founder had spent significant money on advice without corresponding output. The fix was to hire a senior developer who could execute.

## Common mistakes founders make

1. Hiring a fractional CTO when the need is a senior developer.
2. Hiring a senior developer when the need is strategic judgment.
3. Hiring both when one would have sufficed.
4. Not defining the scope of either role.
5. Confusing fractional CTO with engineering management.
6. Treating the fractional CTO as a hands on engineer.
7. No exit terms in either contract.
8. No clear deliverables.

1. **Week one.** Diagnose the actual need. Building or judgment.

3. **Week three.** Trial sprint with the top candidate.
4. **Week four.** Decide. Document the engagement terms.
