## What you need to know

- The most common resume mistake senior engineers make is describing what they were responsible for instead of what they changed. Hiring managers hire for impact, not for job descriptions.
- Listing fifteen years of experience with twenty technologies is less convincing than two years of experience where you can name the architecture decision you made and why it was right.
- AI-assisted screening has made keyword optimization more important than it was two years ago. If the job description mentions specific technologies, your resume should too, assuming you actually know them.
- The resume is not the close. It is the opener. Its job is to get you a call. One page that creates curiosity beats two pages that answer every possible question.
- A public GitHub profile or portfolio that supports your resume claims is worth more than any additional resume bullet point.

## The core argument

Most senior engineers write resumes the way they wrote them as juniors, just with more experience filled in. The junior resume strategy is to show breadth: I know these languages, I have worked in these frameworks, I have contributed to these systems. That strategy makes sense at the start of a career when breadth is your signal. At the senior level, the signal is different. Hiring managers evaluating senior engineers are looking for evidence of judgment, not evidence of exposure. They want to know whether this person has made hard technical decisions under real constraints and whether the outcome was good.

The resume that gets senior engineers callbacks tells a story in each bullet point. Not "developed microservices architecture" but "redesigned the monolith into four services that reduced deployment coupling and cut the average time from code merge to production from three days to forty minutes." That second version answers the question that actually matters: what was the problem, what did you do, and did it work? Every bullet on a strong senior engineer resume should be able to answer those three questions.

The other thing hiring managers read closely is trajectory. Did this person grow into bigger problems over time? Did they move from implementation to architecture to technical leadership? The resume is the map of that trajectory. If all your roles look identical, the narrative reads as someone who is comfortable, not someone who is growing. Every role should show a new class of problem solved, a new scale achieved, or a new leadership challenge taken on. If it does not, either the role did not offer that growth or the resume does not show it. Either way, that is the problem to solve.

## Common mistakes

1. **Writing job descriptions instead of achievements.** Every bullet point that starts with "responsible for" should be rewritten to start with a verb that describes what you did and the outcome that resulted from it.

2. **Including outdated technologies to show breadth.** Listing technologies you learned ten years ago and have not touched since creates confusion. Include only what you would be comfortable discussing in a technical interview today.

3. **Not including metrics or scale.** "Improved database performance" says nothing. "Reduced average query time from 1.2 seconds to 80 milliseconds for a table with 200 million rows" says everything necessary.

4. **Using a generic summary.** "Experienced software engineer with a passion for technology" is on approximately sixty percent of engineering resumes. It is not a summary. It is filler that costs credibility.

5. **Making it too long.** Every year of experience you add is not a reason to add a page. A two-page resume that is dense with impact is better than a three-page resume with padding.

## Where to start

1. **Rewrite your three most recent bullet points using the problem, action, outcome structure.** What was wrong or missing? What did you specifically do? What measurably changed as a result? If you cannot answer all three for a bullet, either dig deeper or cut it.

2. **Cut every technology from the skills section that you cannot speak to at a senior level in a technical interview.** What remains is your actual skill profile. That is the version to present.

3. **Write a two-sentence summary that names your specific focus area, the scale you operate at, and the type of team you work best with.** Specific beats generic every time.
