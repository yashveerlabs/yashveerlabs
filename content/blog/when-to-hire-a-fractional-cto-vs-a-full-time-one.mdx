## What you actually need to know

- Fractional CTO is right when you need senior strategic input a few days a week.
- Full-time CTO is right when daily execution and ownership matter.
- Stage and workload drive the decision. Title prestige does not.
- A strong full-time engineering lead plus a fractional CTO is often the right structure.
- The transition from fractional to full-time happens; agree on the path at the start.

| Stage | Right CTO model |
|---|---|
| Pre-product | Founder-engineer; no CTO yet |
| MVP to first paying customers | Founder-engineer plus advisors |
| 1-10 engineers, finding product-market fit | Fractional CTO if non-technical founder |
| 10-30 engineers, scaling | Full-time CTO or VP Engineering |
| 30+ engineers, growth mode | Full-time CTO, often with VP Engineering reporting |

## The core argument

The fractional CTO has become a useful pattern for early-stage startups whose non-technical founders need senior technical input but cannot justify a full-time senior hire. Done well, it gives the company access to experienced judgment at a fraction of the cost. Done badly, it gives the company a part-time presence who cannot answer the questions that come up daily.

The decision between fractional and full-time should follow the work, not the founder's preference for sounding serious. If the work is strategic and intermittent, fractional fits. If the work is daily execution and ownership, full-time fits. The middle ground, where the work is sometimes both, is the place where companies often try to hire fractional and discover they actually needed full-time.

The other half of the picture is the founder's needs. A non-technical founder who has never hired engineers needs a different kind of help than one who has shipped products before. The first might need a fractional CTO for hiring and architecture review. The second might need an experienced engineering hire to run the team day to day, with no CTO title at all.

Picking by stage and workload, not by org-chart prestige, is the discipline. The companies I have watched overpay for the wrong CTO model usually did so because the founder wanted the title in the deck, not because the work needed it.

## What each model actually does well

### Fractional CTO

Best at: senior pattern recognition applied intermittently. Architecture review. Hiring decisions. Vendor evaluation. Risk identification. Process design. Investor conversations where technical credibility matters. Coaching the founder or the engineering lead.

Bad at: daily execution. Real-time incident response. Detailed code review. Building the team week by week. Anything that requires being there when the question comes up.

### Full-time CTO

Best at: owning the technical direction of the company day to day. Building and leading the engineering team. Driving the architecture. Making real-time decisions during incidents. Being the technical face of the company for investors, customers, and recruits.

Bad at: the company stage where the work is light enough that the role is half-empty. A full-time senior CTO with not enough to do is expensive and often unhappy.

## How much does each cost

| Model | Typical cost | Engagement intensity |
|---|---|---|
| Fractional CTO, 1 day/week | 50k-100k/year | 1 day weekly |
| Fractional CTO, 2 days/week | 100k-200k/year | 2 days weekly |
| Full-time CTO at early stage | 150k-250k cash + equity | Daily |
| Full-time CTO at scale | 300k+ cash + significant equity | Daily, executive load |
| VP Engineering | 200k-350k cash + equity | Daily, often instead of CTO |

The cost numbers vary widely by market and company stage. The shape stays consistent: fractional is cheaper but bounded; full-time is more expensive and unbounded in availability.

## What signals it is time to hire full-time

- The fractional CTO is not there when incidents happen and the team is suffering for it.
- Hiring decisions are stalling because the part-time presence cannot interview enough candidates.
- The engineering team is growing past 10-15 people and needs daily senior presence.
- Investor conversations require deeper technical involvement than the fractional engagement supports.
- The fractional CTO is starting to feel like a bottleneck rather than a multiplier.

## Expert opinion

> The fractional CTO arrangement works beautifully when the work is strategic and intermittent. It fails predictably when the company needs daily ownership. The mistake I see most often is founders hiring fractional because the cost is appealing, then asking the fractional CTO to be present every day, which neither side signed up for. Hire the model the work actually needs, not the model your budget would prefer.
>
> Yashveer Singh Rajpoot, founder of Yashveer Labs

## How this played out on a real project

A non-technical founder asked me to advise on hiring a fractional CTO. We talked through the actual work the role would do. About 60 percent of it was daily: code review, incident response, real-time decisions during outages. About 40 percent was strategic.

I told them honestly that they did not need a fractional CTO. They needed a senior full-stack engineer who could run the team daily, plus an advisor for strategic input. The cash cost was similar but the structure matched the work. They hired both. Two years later the team was stable, the advisor was still helping quarterly, and the founder had not wasted runway on the wrong shape of senior hire. The pattern matches [fractional CTO vs senior full stack developer: which hire saves your runway](/blog/fractional-cto-vs-senior-full-stack-developer-which-hire-saves-your-runway) and the broader [how to hire a full stack developer without getting burned](/blog/how-to-hire-a-full-stack-developer-without-getting-burned).

## Common mistakes

1. Hiring a fractional CTO when the work needs daily presence.
2. Hiring a full-time CTO before the work justifies it.
3. Picking by title prestige rather than by what the role does.
4. Treating fractional as a stepping stone without discussing the transition.
5. Letting a fractional CTO become a bottleneck because they cannot scale their availability.
6. Confusing a fractional CTO with an advisor. The engagement intensity is different.
7. Ignoring references and hiring someone who is fractional in name but absent in practice.

## A 60 day plan to decide and hire

1. **Weeks one and two.** List the work. What does the role actually need to do each week, by hours and by type?
2. **Weeks three and four.** Compare against the fractional and full-time models. Which fits the work?
3. **Weeks five to eight.** Source candidates. References matter. Watch out for fractional CTOs who are essentially busy advisors.
4. **End of two months.** Hire. Define the engagement clearly: hours, scope, decisions they can make, decisions they cannot.
5. **Ongoing.** Review the fit at three months. If the model is wrong, adjust early.
