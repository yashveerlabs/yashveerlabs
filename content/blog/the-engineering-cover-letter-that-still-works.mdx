## What you actually need to know

- A cover letter that summarizes the resume is a waste of both parties' time. Use it to make an argument the resume cannot.
- The hiring manager's question is: does this person understand the problem we are trying to solve? Answer that question.
- Specificity about the company signals genuine interest, which itself is a differentiating signal when most candidates are generic.
- Three paragraphs is the right length. Not two, not five. Three.
- The final paragraph should have a specific call to action, not "I look forward to hearing from you."

| Cover Letter Element | Purpose | Common Mistake |
|---|---|---|
| Opening line | Establish why you are writing | Starting with "I am applying for..." |
| Company-specific hook | Signal you researched them | Generic compliment ("great culture") |
| Relevant experience | Connect your work to their problem | Listing without connecting |
| Closing | Request a specific next step | Passive "I hope to hear from you" |

## The core argument

The cover letter has been declared dead so many times that most engineers do not write one or write one so generic it functions as noise. The result is that a specific, well-constructed cover letter has become a differentiating signal because the bar for differentiation has dropped to zero. An engineer who writes a cover letter that demonstrates they understand the company's technical challenges, references specific things that signal research, and connects past work to current needs is standing in a field of candidates who wrote nothing or wrote variations of "I am an experienced engineer passionate about technology."

The cover letter works when it is not trying to cover everything. The resume covers the facts of your career. The cover letter makes one argument: here is why I am the right person for this specific problem. One argument, not five. The hiring manager reading the cover letter is trying to decide whether to spend more time on this candidate. The letter needs to answer that question quickly and specifically.

I have seen cover letters that were more effective than the resume at moving the process forward. They were effective because they demonstrated something the resume could not: the candidate had thought seriously about the company's situation and had relevant experience they could articulate in the context of that situation. This is a skill. It takes practice. The engineers who develop it have a structural advantage in every competitive hiring process.

The template approach to cover letters -- the "insert company name here" format that is standard advice -- produces letters that hiring managers can identify as templates immediately. The specificity that makes a cover letter work cannot be templated. It comes from actually researching the company: reading their engineering blog, understanding their product, knowing their current technical challenges from their job description and public communications. This takes an hour per application. It is worth it for the applications you care about.

## What research looks like in practice

The cover letter research that produces useful content: the company's engineering blog (what problems are they solving? what technology choices have they made?), recent product announcements (what are they building?), the job description (what specific technical problem prompted this hire?), and any public information about the team's challenges (conference talks by their engineers, technical blog posts, GitHub activity if they are open source).

From this research, identify one specific technical area where your experience is directly relevant. Not your entire career. One specific thing. If their engineering blog has a post about database scaling challenges and you have spent two years working on database optimization, that is the connection. If the job description emphasizes real-time data processing and you have built real-time pipelines, that is the connection. Find it and make it explicit in the cover letter.

The cover letter that works opens by naming the specific technical problem the company is working on (not a generic description of the company), presents two to three sentences of directly relevant past experience with specifics, and closes by naming what you want to happen next. This structure takes the guesswork out of evaluation. The hiring manager reads it and either thinks "yes, this person gets it" or "this is not what we need." Both outcomes are good -- one advances the process, one saves both parties time.

## Writing the letter

The opening line should not be "I am applying for the Senior Backend Engineer position." That is stated in the application itself. The opening line should demonstrate you have something to say. Something like: "Your post on handling 10 million daily active users on a single-region Postgres cluster mirrors a scaling challenge we worked through at [your company] last year, and I want to walk you through how we approached it."

The middle paragraph connects your most relevant past experience to their specific problem. Be concrete. Use numbers where available. Reference specific technical decisions, not just outcomes. "We migrated from a monolithic Rails application to a service-oriented architecture over 18 months, and the failure mode we most needed to avoid was distributed transaction complexity -- here is how we handled it" is more useful to the hiring manager than "I have experience with microservices."

The closing paragraph should state what you want. Not "I hope to be considered" -- that is not a call to action. "I would like 30 minutes to walk through the architecture decisions we made and how they might apply to what you are building" is a call to action. It proposes a specific next step and frames the meeting as mutually valuable, which it is.

## Common mistakes engineers make with cover letters

1. Writing a paragraph-form version of the resume. The hiring manager has the resume. Do not repeat it in prose. Use the cover letter to add information the resume cannot convey.
2. Making the cover letter about your career goals rather than their problem. "I am looking for an opportunity to grow in distributed systems" tells the hiring manager about your needs. The cover letter should tell them about how you address theirs.
3. Using generic praise of the company. "I have followed [Company] for years and am inspired by your mission to transform the industry" is detectable as template text. It signals that no research was done.
4. Making it too long. Five paragraphs covering your entire career is not a cover letter -- it is an essay. The longer it is, the more you have asked the reader to work before getting to the point.
5. Not writing one at all. For applications where you have a genuine connection to the technical problem, the cover letter is often what moves the process forward. Not writing one when you could is leaving a differentiating tool on the table.

## Where to start: a 3-step cover letter approach

**Step 1: Do 45 minutes of specific research on the company.** Read their engineering blog, their recent job descriptions, and any public technical content their team has produced. Write two sentences summarizing the technical problem that prompted this hire.

**Step 2: Identify the single most relevant experience in your background for that problem.** Not the most impressive experience generally. The most directly relevant experience for what they are working on right now. This is the centerpiece of the cover letter.

**Step 3: Write three paragraphs using the structure above.** Opening that names their problem, middle that connects your experience to it specifically, close that proposes a concrete next step. Read it once and cut any sentence that would be equally true of someone else applying for this role.
