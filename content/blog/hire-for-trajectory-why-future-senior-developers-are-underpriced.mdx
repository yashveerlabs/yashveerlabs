- Hire the engineer about to become senior at mid level cost.
- Behaviors and work product predict the next title.
- Writing, mentorship, ownership, and curiosity are the signs.
- Risk is bounded. Upside is meaningful.
- Find them through their public work, not just their resume.

## The core argument

The hiring market prices titles. The recruiter scans the resume. The mid level engineer gets mid level offers. The senior engineer gets senior offers. The system works for most engineers. The system misprices the engineer about to become senior.

The engineer about to become senior has the resume of a mid level engineer. They have the title of a mid level engineer. They have the behaviors of a senior engineer. The behaviors are not visible in the resume scan. The recruiter passes them over for the senior role. The hiring manager who interviews them sees the trajectory and either captures it at mid level cost or hires the senior at senior cost.

The teams that hire for trajectory deliberately capture engineers the market is mispricing. The engineer accepts a mid level offer because the title is mid level. The team gets senior work within twelve months. The engineer gets a promotion within twelve to eighteen months. Both sides win.

The behaviors that predict trajectory are specific. Strong writing. Active mentorship. Ownership of operational work. Curiosity about adjacent surfaces. Public contributions outside the current employer. Pushback on scope with deliberate reasoning. Each is a behavior pattern, not a credential.

The mistake teams make is hiring on resume alone. The resume tells you about the past. The trajectory tells you about the future. The hiring decision is about the future. The teams that read past the resume capture value that the market is missing.

## The behaviors that signal trajectory

| Behavior | Detail |
|---|---|
| Public writing | Blog posts, conference talks, internal memos that get shared |
| Mentorship | Volunteers to mentor more junior engineers |
| Operational ownership | Takes on call. Writes runbooks. Leads incident response |
| Curiosity | Reads code outside their team. Asks how other surfaces work |
| Open source | Contributes to projects they did not start |
| Scope pushback | Deliberately argues about what should be in a project |
| Hard questions | Asks the questions in design reviews that others miss |
| Honesty about gaps | Admits what they do not know. Names the trade off. |

## Features the trajectory hire must have

- A trial sprint that reveals the behaviors.
- Reference checks that ask about trajectory specifically.
- A documented growth plan post hire.
- A path to promotion within twelve to eighteen months.
- Compensation that adjusts as the title catches up.
- A manager who develops the engineer.

## How this played out for engineers I have worked with

A team I advised was deciding between two candidates. One had a senior title from a known company at 280k USD comp expectation. The other had a mid level title at 165k USD comp. The mid level candidate had strong public writing, mentored juniors, and asked deeper questions in the interview.

We hired the mid level candidate. Within eight months the engineer was producing senior level work. Within fourteen months the promotion landed. The total compensation in the second year matched the senior level. The first year savings were over 100k USD. The engineer would not have been available at the senior level twelve months earlier because the title had not caught up.

The senior candidate would have produced senior work from day one but at much higher cost. The trajectory candidate produced mid level work for the first six months and senior work for the rest. The total output across the year was comparable. The cost difference was meaningful.

## Common mistakes teams make

1. Hiring on resume alone.
2. Confusing confidence with trajectory.
3. No trial sprint that reveals behaviors.
4. No growth plan post hire.
5. Compensation that does not catch up as the title catches up.
6. No manager development. Trajectory stalls.
7. Treating trajectory hires as cheaper instead of as the same value at different cost.
8. Not reading the engineer's public work.

## A 30 day evaluation framework

1. **Week one.** Source candidates. Read their public work.
2. **Week two.** Initial interviews. Look for the behavior signals.
3. **Week three.** Trial sprint with the top candidates. Evaluate behaviors directly.
4. **Week four.** Decide. Document the growth plan.
