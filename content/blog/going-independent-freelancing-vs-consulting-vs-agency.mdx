## What you actually need to know

- Freelancing is the lightest path. Hours for dollars.
- Consulting is the highest leverage solo path. Expertise for premium rates.
- Agency adds team. Leverage and complexity both grow.
- Most engineers should start freelancing and transition deliberately.
- Underpricing in year one is the most common trap.

| Path | Rate range | Operational complexity |
|---|---|---|
| Freelance | 100 to 250 USD per hour | Low |
| Consulting | 250 to 1000 USD per hour | Low to medium |
| Solo agency | Variable | Medium |
| Multi person agency | Variable | High |
| Productized service | Variable | Medium |

## The core argument

Going independent is a real career transition that engineers make at different points in their lives. The right path depends on the person and the stage. The wrong path wastes the years spent on it. The right path compounds over a decade.

Freelancing is the lightest path. The engineer sells hours of engineering work to clients. The barrier to entry is low. The skills required are mostly engineering plus enough business to find clients, write contracts, and invoice. Most engineers can start freelancing within a few months of deciding to.

Consulting is the higher leverage solo path. The consultant sells expertise and judgment rather than hours. The rates are higher because the value per hour is higher. The clients buy because the consultant solves a specific problem that the client cannot solve themselves. The transition from freelancing to consulting is the development of a specific brand that the client recognizes.

Agency adds a team. The leverage on revenue is real because the team multiplies output. The complexity is also real. People management. Operations. Consistent delivery across the team. The agency owner does less engineering and more business. The skills required are different from the consultant or freelancer.

The pattern that works for most engineers is to start with freelancing, transition to consulting as the brand develops, and consider agency only if the operational appetite matches. The jump from engineer directly to agency usually fails because the sales, operations, and people skills have not been built.

## The decision map

| Question | Freelance | Consulting | Agency |
|---|---|---|---|

## Features the independent setup must have

- Personal brand and portfolio.
- Clear positioning of what you do.
- A contract template.
- An invoicing system.
- A pipeline tracker for leads.
- An accountant on retainer.
- Insurance appropriate to the jurisdiction.
- A clear path from current state to the next level.

## Expert opinion

> Most engineers should start freelancing rather than jumping directly to consulting or agency. The skills compound. The brand develops. The transitions to higher leverage paths happen naturally as the work develops. The engineers who jump directly to agency usually struggle because the operational and people skills have not been built. The engineers who start freelancing and transition deliberately end up where they wanted to be without the early failures.

## Common mistakes engineers make

1. Underpricing in year one.
2. Jumping to agency without the operational skills.
3. No clear positioning.
4. No contract template. Disputes follow.
5. No accountant. Tax season punishes.
6. No insurance.
7. Hiding from sales conversations. Pipeline starves.
8. Treating going independent as solved once started.

## A 90 day independent launch plan

1. **Month one.** Set up the legal entity, contract, invoicing, accounting. Define the positioning.
2. **Month two.** Build the brand artifacts. Personal site. LinkedIn. Portfolio. Reach out to network.
3. **Month three.** Land the first client. Deliver well. Use the work for the next pitch.
