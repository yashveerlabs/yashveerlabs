## What you actually need to know

- Most plateaus are caused by impact scope, not technical skill. The path to the next level is usually more scope, not more skill.
- The engineer who waits to be given scope will wait for a long time. Create the impact without the title.
- A direct conversation with the manager about specific promotion criteria is more productive than a general conversation about career growth.
- Changing companies can break a plateau, but it changes the timeline rather than the problem if the underlying capability gap remains.
- The engineer who is plateaued and complaining is less promotable than the engineer who is plateaued and actively working on the gap.

| Plateau Type | Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Technical skill gap | Not enough depth at next level | Specific skill development over 6 months |
| Scope gap | Not operating at next level's scope | Take on broader projects, create scope |
| Visibility gap | Impact not seen by decision-makers | Communicate impact more explicitly |
| Structural | No open position at next level | Change companies or wait for the position |
| Performance gap | Inconsistent performance | Performance improvement, then promotion conversation |

## The core argument

The engineer who has been a senior engineer for three years and has not been promoted to staff is almost never failing to write good code. They are almost always failing to create impact at the scope required for staff. The distinction matters because the solution is different.

Adding more technical skill when the gap is scope is solving the wrong problem. The technical skill at senior is sufficient for staff. What changes at staff is not the technical ceiling but the radius of impact. Staff engineers are not better coders than senior engineers. They are engineers who make the people around them more effective, who make architectural decisions that improve the whole system, and who operate effectively at a scope that includes multiple teams and multiple stakeholders.

The plateau usually becomes visible to the engineer before it becomes visible to the manager. The engineer notices that they are performing well but not advancing. The manager, who is busy, may not have thought deeply about what the gap is. The productive move is to force the specificity: ask the manager to map the gap to the specific criteria for the next level. Not "what do I need to do to get promoted?" but "here is how I believe I meet the criteria for staff. Where do you disagree?"

This conversation is uncomfortable because it requires both parties to be specific. It is the conversation that breaks the plateau or reveals that the plateau is structural (no staff opening exists, and the company is too small to create one). Either outcome is useful information.

## Creating scope without being given it

The most common reason engineers wait years for a promotion that would come from taking on broader impact is that they are waiting for someone to give them the scope. They are waiting to be assigned the architectural project, to be made the tech lead, to be given the mentorship responsibility. The assignment rarely comes until the impact has already been demonstrated.

The engineers who break out of plateaus create scope from what is available. They notice a documentation gap and fix it without being asked. They identify a recurring support issue and build the internal tool that eliminates it. They notice that a junior engineer is struggling and start a weekly pairing session. They write the architecture document for a system that lacks one. None of these require permission. All of them demonstrate staff-level behavior.

The impact that leads to promotion is often invisible to the manager until it is pointed out. The engineer who built the internal tool needs to make sure the engineering leader knows it was built, how it works, and how much time it saves the team. The impact needs to be communicated, not just created.

## Knowing when to change companies

Changing companies can break a career plateau, but it changes the context rather than the capability. An engineer who is plateaued because of a technical skill gap is plateaued at the next company too, unless they develop the skill during the transition period. An engineer who is plateaued because the current company has no staff opening can find one at a company that does.

The productive analysis: is the plateau caused by my performance or by the structural limitations of the current company? If the company has a staff engineer position that needs to be filled and they are not considering you for it, the gap is in your performance or visibility. If the company does not have a staff engineer position, changing companies is the direct path to the title.

The risk of changing companies too quickly: the new company hires based on the new level, but the engineer has not demonstrated the capability in a context where it is observable. The first six months at the new company are spent proving it. If the capability is not there, the promotion does not follow the title.

## Common mistakes plateaued engineers make

1. Waiting for the gap to be identified rather than diagnosing it themselves. The engineer who does not know why they are not being promoted is not going to be promoted until they understand the gap.
2. Improving in the wrong dimension. More technical depth when the gap is scope, or more scope when the gap is consistency of execution.
3. Not communicating impact. Creating impact that the manager does not know about is creating impact that does not count for promotion decisions.
4. Treating the promotion conversation as a performance review rather than a planning conversation. The promotion conversation should happen monthly, not annually.
5. Accepting vague feedback. "You're doing great but not quite ready" is not feedback. The specific behavior or impact that is missing is the information required to make progress.

## Where to start: a 3-step plateau diagnosis

**Step 1: Map your current work to the criteria for the next level.** Be specific. For each criterion, identify whether you meet it and what evidence you have. The gaps that appear in this mapping are the actual problem.

**Step 2: Schedule a direct promotion criteria conversation with your manager.** Share your self-assessment. Ask for their evaluation of each criterion. Ask what specifically would change their assessment on the criteria you do not currently meet.

**Step 3: Create one initiative per month that addresses the identified gap.** If the gap is scope, identify a cross-team problem to lead. If the gap is mentorship, identify an engineer to invest in. If the gap is architectural influence, write a proposal for a system improvement and present it to the team.
