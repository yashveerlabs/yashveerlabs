## What you need to know

- Hiring under urgency is the most expensive way to hire. The pipeline exists so you are never hiring under urgency.
- The best developers do not apply to job posts. They get hired through relationships, direct outreach, and referrals. Your pipeline needs sources for each.
- A pre-vetted shortlist of two to five engineers you could call today is worth more than a stack of unreviewed applications.
- The evaluation process needs to be fast enough to compete with other opportunities the candidate is considering. Slow processes lose good candidates.
- Every hire you make is a seed for the next one. Developers who do good work with you know other developers who do good work. Ask before they leave.

## The core argument

The bootstrapped founder who waits to build a hiring pipeline until they need a developer is in the same position as a company that waits to build a sales pipeline until they need revenue. The urgency that drives the hire also degrades the quality of the decision. You take the first candidate who seems competent because you do not have the time or the alternatives to be selective. That pattern is the root cause of most early-stage hiring mistakes.

The alternative is to maintain a lightweight, ongoing process that keeps two things alive: a warm network of engineers who know your work and would respond to a message, and a short pre-vetted list of developers who have passed at least the initial evaluation in your process. This does not require a recruiter. It requires thirty minutes a week of deliberate relationship maintenance and a clear, documented evaluation process that you can run quickly when a hire becomes necessary.

The channels that consistently yield the best developer pipeline for bootstrapped founders are not the ones that cost the most. In my experience, the highest quality sources are warm referrals from engineers you have worked with before, direct outreach to developers whose public work you have followed for a specific reason, and communities where engineers show their work openly. A developer who has published a useful open-source tool, written a detailed technical post, or contributed to a community discussion has demonstrated multiple qualities at once: technical skill, communication ability, and the discipline to finish and share something. That combination is worth more than any resume.

## Common mistakes

1. **Only sourcing candidates when a role is open.** The best candidates are always slightly unavailable. The pipeline works because you have been building it for months before you need it.

2. **Relying exclusively on job posts.** Job posts attract candidates who are actively looking. The best developers are usually not actively looking. They move on referral, relationship, and direct outreach.

3. **Not documenting your evaluation process.** An undocumented process is a new process every time. Write down the steps, the criteria, and the decision rules. Running the same process twice produces consistent data.

4. **Losing good candidates to slow processes.** A developer who interviews well and is ready to work will take the first suitable offer they receive. If your process takes six weeks, you are not competing.

5. **Not asking good developers for referrals.** The best closing question for any developer engagement is: who else would you hire without hesitation? This question alone builds the most reliable pipeline available to a bootstrapped founder.

## Where to start

1. **Identify three developers you have worked with or followed closely and send each a short message.** Not a job offer. A genuine check-in or a specific question about their work. This is the first step in building a warm network with intention.

2. **Write your evaluation process as a one-page document.** The steps from first contact to trial decision, the criteria at each step, and the approximate time each step takes. Now you have a repeatable system instead of a new approach each time.

3. **Add a standing task to your weekly review to do one pipeline activity.** Following a developer's work, sharing a relevant article, or sending one specific message. Thirty minutes a week for six months builds a network that transforms your hiring options.
