## What you actually need to know

- Real mentorship has agendas, homework, and measurable progress.
- The mentee brings specific questions. The mentor brings specific advice.
- Biweekly meetings are typical. Monthly is the minimum.
- Six months to two years is a normal arc.
- Ending the formal relationship cleanly is a feature.

## The core argument

Engineering mentorship is one of those things that sounds great in concept and produces nothing in practice for most relationships. The mentor and mentee meet. They have pleasant conversations about career. They leave with nothing changed. The pattern is common because the easy version is comfortable and produces no growth.

The mentorship that works is more specific. The mentee brings concrete questions. The mentor gives concrete advice. The mentee acts on the advice. The mentor sees the result. The cycle compounds. Six months in the mentee is measurably stronger than they would have been alone.

The discipline that makes it work is on both sides. The mentee has to bring specifics. The vague conversation is not useful. The mentor has to engage with specifics. Generic career advice is not useful either. Both sides have to commit to the work between meetings. Without the work the meetings drift.

The mentor's contribution is pattern recognition. The mentor has been through this. The mentor recognizes when the mentee is overthinking a decision or underthinking one. The mentor can name the trade off the mentee is missing. The advice is specific to the situation.

The mentee's contribution is action. The mentor's best advice is wasted if the mentee does not act on it. The mentee who tries the suggestion and reports back drives the relationship forward. The mentee who hears the suggestion and ignores it stalls.

## The structure that works

| Element | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Biweekly meetings | Cadence that maintains momentum |
| Agendas from mentee | Forces specifics |
| Forty five to sixty minutes | Long enough for depth |
| Homework between meetings | Action follows advice |
| Quarterly reflection | Measure progress |
| Defined arc | Six months to two years |
| Clean ending | Formal end, informal continuation |
| Multiple mentors | Different mentors for different topics |

## How much does this cost

The cost is time. Two to four hours per month per side. The cost is real but small relative to the career impact of a strong mentorship. The cost of theater mentorship is the same time without the return.

## Features the mentorship must have

- A clear agreement on goals and cadence.
- A meeting structure with agendas.
- Homework or action items between meetings.
- A way for the mentor to see the mentee's actual work.
- A quarterly reflection on progress.
- A defined arc with an ending.
- A way to end cleanly if the relationship is not working.
- Permission for the mentor to push back.

## How this is playing out for me

I am running this in both directions. I have a few mentors who give me perspective I cannot get from peers at my stage. I also mentor a few junior engineers. The patterns are the same in both directions. The mentorships that work have specifics, agendas, and homework. The ones that drift do not.

The mentorships that have helped me most were the ones where the mentor pushed back. Generic encouragement was less useful than specific criticism. The mentors who told me when I was overthinking something or underestimating something shaped my judgment faster than the ones who just supported me.

The mentees I have worked with who grew fastest were the ones who acted on the advice. They tried the suggestion. They came back with results. The conversation moved forward. The mentees who heard advice and did not act on it stalled. The pattern is on the mentee side as much as on the mentor side.

## Common mistakes pairs make

1. No agenda for meetings.
2. Vague topics from the mentee.
3. Generic advice from the mentor.
4. No homework between meetings.
5. No quarterly reflection.
6. No defined arc. Drifts forever.
7. Mentor lectures instead of engaging.
8. Mentee does not act on advice.

## A starting framework

1. **Meeting one.** Agree on goals. Set the cadence. Define what success looks like.
2. **Meetings two to four.** The mentee brings specific topics. The mentor engages. Homework follows.
3. **Quarterly.** Reflect on progress. Adjust goals.
4. **End of arc.** Formal ending. Informal continuation if desired.
