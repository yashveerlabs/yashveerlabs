## What you actually need to know

- The default choice should be a commercial CRM. Salesforce, HubSpot, and Pipedrive handle 90 percent of sales and customer management needs well. Building custom is the exception, not the default.
- The custom CRM pays off when the commercial CRM requires customization that approaches the cost of the custom build. The threshold is typically when the commercial CRM requires 200+ hours of implementation work to fit the business's workflow.
- Scope the custom CRM tightly. The 20 percent of a commercial CRM that the team actually uses is the right scope for a first build. Not a feature-complete Salesforce replacement.
- The ongoing maintenance cost of a custom CRM is higher than the subscription cost of a commercial one, and grows with every feature added.
- The build-vs-buy calculation should include a 3-year total cost comparison, not just the upfront costs.

| CRM Option | Year 1 Cost | Year 2-3 Cost | Customization | Maintenance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HubSpot Starter | $4,800/year | $4,800/year | Limited | Vendor-managed |
| Salesforce with customization | $15,000-60,000 first year | $12,000-25,000/year | Extensive | Vendor + your team |

| Custom CRM (full-featured) | $80,000-200,000 build | $15,000-40,000/year | Unlimited | Your team only |

## The core argument

The decision to build a custom CRM is most often a reaction to the frustration of working with a commercial CRM that does not quite fit the business. The Salesforce implementation that has been customized to the point where the original product is unrecognizable. The HubSpot pipeline that requires 10 workarounds to match the actual sales process. The Pipedrive account that has been cluttered with unused fields because every sales process tweak requires a new custom field.

These frustrations are real and the desire for something that fits the business exactly is understandable. The calculation that is often not done: the cost of the custom build and its ongoing maintenance, compared to the total cost of fixing the commercial CRM's fit problems.

In most cases, the fit problems can be addressed with additional commercial CRM customization, a CRM migration to a better-fitting product, or process changes that accommodate the commercial CRM's assumptions. The custom build is justified when none of these options produces a total cost lower than the custom build path.

The specific conditions where custom beats commercial:

**Non-standard relationship models.** Commercial CRMs assume a lead-to-contact-to-deal structure with organizations and activities. Industries with relationship models that do not fit this structure -- real estate (property-centric relationships), healthcare (patient-centric with complex compliance requirements), staffing (candidate and employer relationships simultaneously) -- often need a data model that commercial CRMs cannot accommodate without excessive abstraction.

**Deep internal system integration.** A business whose CRM data must flow bidirectionally with a proprietary internal system (a custom ERP, a manufacturing system, a legacy database) may find that commercial CRM integration capabilities are insufficient. If the integration requires extensive custom middleware anyway, the gap between the commercial CRM and a custom build narrows.

**Team-specific workflow automation.** Businesses with highly specific workflow automation requirements -- complex routing logic, multi-step approval processes, domain-specific triggers -- sometimes find that commercial CRM automation tools (Salesforce Process Builder, HubSpot Workflows) require elaborate workarounds. At some point, the workaround complexity justifies a custom implementation.

## What to build in a custom CRM (and what not to)

The mistake in most custom CRM projects is scope. Teams describe what they want as "a CRM like Salesforce but customized for us." Salesforce has hundreds of engineers and decades of development behind it. The custom CRM has 3-6 weeks of development time. The result of trying to replicate Salesforce is a half-finished product that is worse than both Salesforce and a simpler custom build.

The right scope for a first custom CRM: the features the team uses daily, not the features the commercial CRM has. For most businesses, this is:

- Contact/company database with the specific fields that matter for this business (not generic name/email/phone, but the domain-specific attributes)
- Activity log with timestamped notes, calls, emails, and meetings, associated with contacts
- Pipeline view showing contacts at each stage of the relationship process
- Assignment to team members with visibility into who owns which relationships
- Search and filter capabilities that support the team's actual workflow

This is 3-6 weeks of development. It is not a complete CRM replacement; it is the core that the team will actually use every day. The remaining features (email integration, reporting dashboards, mobile app, bulk operations) are added after the core is validated and used.

## The build timeline and team requirement

A custom CRM at the minimal scope described above requires one full-stack developer for 4-6 weeks. The stack should be boring and maintainable: a standard relational database (PostgreSQL), a server-rendered or API-backed web application, and a UI framework the developer knows well. This is not the place to experiment with new technology.

The data model design is the most important week of the project. The entities the custom CRM stores and the relationships between them define what the CRM can do. Getting this wrong requires expensive migrations later. Invest a full day in data model design with the team that will use the CRM before writing any code.

The launch should be to a limited set of users (2-3 team members) who will use it daily and provide feedback. The feedback from daily use in the first month will shape the second phase of development more accurately than any upfront specification.

## Common mistakes in custom CRM projects

1. Building Salesforce features instead of the team's actual workflow. Every hour spent building features that are "in Salesforce" but not actually used is an hour that is not building the core the team needs.
2. Not involving the sales or account management team in the data model design. The people who will use the CRM daily understand the domain better than the developer. Their input on the data model prevents the "this does not fit how we actually work" problem at launch.
3. Not planning for data migration from the commercial CRM. If the team is moving from HubSpot to custom, the existing contacts and activities must be migrated. This migration is a significant project that is often underestimated.
4. Not budgeting for ongoing development. The custom CRM will receive feature requests from the day of launch. Budget for 10-15 percent of the build cost per year in ongoing development, separate from maintenance.
5. Building the CRM before validating the process. A team that is still figuring out its sales process should not build a custom CRM -- the process will change, and the CRM will be built for the old process. Stabilize the process first, then build the tool.

## Where to start: a 3-step build-vs-buy evaluation

**Step 1: Document the commercial CRM features you actually use weekly.** Not the features that are available, but the ones the team uses. The list is typically 15-25 percent of the commercial CRM's feature set.

**Step 2: List every workflow where the commercial CRM requires a workaround.** For each workaround, estimate the time it costs the team weekly. Multiply by the loaded hourly cost of the team member. This is the annual cost of the commercial CRM's fit problems.

**Step 3: Compare the annual fit problem cost to the cost of a custom CRM build.** If the annual fit cost exceeds the build cost in 2-3 years, the custom build has positive expected value. If it does not, continue with the commercial CRM or address the specific fit problems with additional customization.
