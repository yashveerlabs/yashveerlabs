## What you actually need to know

- Three to six weeks of focused preparation is usually enough for senior engineers.
- Targeted preparation matched to the specific interviews beats volume.
- 100 to 150 LeetCode problems with depth beats 500 with shallow practice.
- System design and behavioral preparation matter as much as coding.
- Rest matters. The exhausted engineer fails interviews they would pass rested.

| Preparation type | Time investment |
|---|---|
| Coding fundamentals | 1 to 3 weeks |
| System design | 1 to 2 weeks |
| Behavioral stories | A few days, refined over the prep |
| Company specific | A few hours per company |
| Mock interviews | 4 to 8 across the prep |
| Rest and recovery | Daily |

## The core argument

Engineering interview preparation has a default mode that is volume based and burnout inducing. The engineer reads internet advice. The advice says grind LeetCode for six months. The engineer grinds. The engineer arrives at the interview exhausted. The exhaustion costs the clarity that the interview actually rewards. The engineer fails interviews they would have passed with less preparation and more rest.

The better approach is targeted preparation. The engineer identifies the specific companies and roles they are interviewing for. The engineer studies the formats those companies use. The engineer practices the specific kinds of questions those companies ask. The total preparation is three to six weeks of focused work rather than months of grinding.

The depth matters more than the count. A hundred problems studied deeply produces better pattern recognition than five hundred problems grinded superficially. The engineer who understands why the binary search worked on this problem can apply binary search to a new problem. The engineer who memorized the solution to this problem cannot.

The preparation includes system design and behavioral interviews. The engineer who only prepared coding will struggle at the senior level where these matter more. The engineer who only prepared behavioral will struggle at the technical bar. The right preparation covers all three layers.

Rest is the lever most engineers ignore. The exhausted engineer performs worse than they would rested. The engineer who sleeps eight hours, exercises, and takes days off arrives at the interview clear. The clarity is what the interview rewards.

## The preparation plan

| Week | Focus |
|---|---|
| Week one | Coding fundamentals review. Patterns. |
| Week two | LeetCode practice on the relevant pattern categories |
| Week three | System design fundamentals and practice |
| Week four | Behavioral story refinement, mock interviews |
| Week five | Company specific preparation, more mocks |
| Week six | Rest and final polish |

## How much does this cost

The cost is time. Three to six weeks of dedicated preparation if not employed. Two hours per evening plus weekend mornings if employed. The total is roughly 80 to 200 hours of preparation. The return is the offer. The math depends on the role's compensation but is usually favorable for any meaningful move.

## Features the preparation must have

- A targeted list of companies and roles.
- A practice plan that covers coding, system design, and behavioral.
- A mock interview schedule.
- A time bound on the preparation.
- Daily rest discipline.
- A reference list of resources used.
- A way to track problems studied and patterns learned.
- A debrief process after each interview.

## Expert opinion

> The engineers who land the best offers prepared deliberately, not exhaustively. They studied the specific formats. They practiced the specific patterns. They rested. The engineers who burned out grinding produced their worst performances at the interviews they cared about most. The discipline is to prepare with intention and to stop when the time has come.
>
> Yashveer Singh Rajpoot, founder of Yashveer Labs

## How this played out for engineers I have advised

The pattern across engineers I have advised on interview preparation is consistent. The engineer comes in with a vague plan and four months of intended grinding. We restructure to a six week targeted plan. The engineer practices fewer problems with more depth. The engineer prepares system design and behavioral seriously. The engineer rests.

The interview results are consistently better than the engineer's previous attempts. The depth of pattern recognition shows up in unfamiliar problems. The behavioral stories land because they were practiced specifically for the company's values. The clarity from rest produces better communication than the grinder version of the same engineer.

The pattern works because the interview rewards clarity, judgment, and depth. Volume of practice does not produce these. Targeted practice with rest does.

## Common mistakes engineers make

1. Grinding for months without targeting.
2. No system design preparation for senior roles.
3. No behavioral preparation.
4. Volume over depth.
5. No rest in the preparation schedule.
6. No mock interviews.
7. Studying generic LeetCode lists without matching to target companies.
8. Treating the interview as a pure technical test when it tests communication too.

## A six week preparation plan

1. **Week one.** Identify the target companies. Map their interview formats. Build the practice plan.
2. **Week two.** Coding fundamentals. The patterns that match the target companies.
3. **Week three.** System design. Read. Practice with a partner.
4. **Week four.** Behavioral stories. Refine each one. Practice telling.
5. **Week five.** Mock interviews. Company specific preparation.
6. **Week six.** Rest. Final polish. Sleep before the actual interviews.
