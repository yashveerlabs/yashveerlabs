## What you actually need to know

- Most online cost calculators are lead generation. The numbers do not match reality.
- The honest method is to break the project into components and apply rates.
- Contingency of 25 to 35 percent is not optional.
- Ongoing costs after launch are 20 to 30 percent of build annually.
- Trust no incentive sources, not the agency calculator.

| Component | Typical effort | US senior rate | Component cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authentication | 2 to 4 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 16k to 32k USD |
| Onboarding flow | 3 to 6 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 24k to 48k USD |
| Core workflow | 8 to 20 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 64k to 160k USD |
| Payment | 2 to 4 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 16k to 32k USD |
| Admin tools | 3 to 8 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 24k to 64k USD |
| Notifications | 2 to 4 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 16k to 32k USD |
| Reports | 3 to 6 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 24k to 48k USD |
| Mobile app | 12 to 24 weeks | 200 USD per hour | 96k to 192k USD |

## The core argument

The cost calculator on the internet looks helpful. The founder enters a few details about the project. The calculator returns a number. The founder uses the number to plan. The number is almost always wrong.

The reason is incentives. The calculators are built by agencies as lead generation tools. The number is the bait. The goal is to make the founder schedule a call. The calculator does not benefit from being accurate. It benefits from being engaging. The two are not the same.

The honest way to estimate cost is to break the project into known components. Each component has a known effort range. Each engineer level has a known rate range. The multiplication produces a number that is much closer to reality than the calculator output.

The discipline is in being honest about the components. The founder who thinks the project is just a few screens is missing authentication, payment, admin, notifications, reports, and mobile. The honest breakdown surfaces the actual scope. The cost follows the scope.

The contingency is the part most founders skip. The unknown unknowns are real. A project without contingency runs over or cuts scope. The contingency funds the response to the surprises. Without it, the project either compromises on quality or runs out of money.

## How to estimate without the calculator

Step one. List every component honestly. Authentication, onboarding, core workflow, payment, admin, notifications, reports, mobile. Most projects need at least the first five.

Step two. Estimate effort per component in engineer weeks. The ranges are documented above. Pick the middle for your first estimate.

Step three. Multiply by the realistic rate for the engineer level you will hire. The hourly rate times forty hours per week times the number of weeks.

Step four. Add 25 to 35 percent contingency.

Step five. Add infrastructure, third party services, design, content, and security as separate line items. These are often equal to the development cost in the second year.

The result is a much more accurate estimate than the calculator produced.

## How much do calculators typically underestimate

| Calculator output | Actual cost ratio |
|---|---|
| Agency lead gen calculator | Often 0.4 to 0.7 of actual |
| Optimistic timeline tools | Often 0.5 to 0.7 of actual |
| Self serve estimators | Often 0.6 to 0.9 of actual |
| Component breakdown from honest estimation | Roughly accurate |

## Features the estimate must have

- Component breakdown with named items.
- Effort in engineer weeks per component.
- Rate aligned to the engineer level.
- Contingency.
- Infrastructure and third party line items.
- Maintenance line for year two.
- A clear range, not a single number.

## How this played out on a real project

A founder came to me with a project plan based on an online cost calculator. The calculator had suggested 65k USD for a B2B SaaS MVP. The founder had budgeted accordingly. The realistic estimate for the actual scope was closer to 180k USD.

We did the component breakdown together. Authentication, onboarding, core workflow, payment, admin, notifications. Each component took weeks of work. The math came out to roughly 165k USD before contingency. With contingency, 210k USD.

The founder was disappointed but listened. They restructured the fundraise. They cut some scope. They built a realistic 175k USD plan. The MVP shipped on schedule and on budget. The alternative plan based on the calculator would have run out of money in month three with nothing to show.

## Common mistakes founders make

1. Trusting the calculator output.
2. Skipping the component breakdown.
3. Underestimating the long components like core workflow.
4. Ignoring the contingency.
5. Treating infrastructure as rounding error.
6. Forgetting maintenance in year two.
7. Pricing at junior engineer rates and hiring senior engineers.
8. Treating a single number estimate as accurate.

## A one day estimation exercise

1. **Hour one.** List the components honestly.
2. **Hour two.** Estimate effort per component.
3. **Hour three.** Apply the rate for the engineer level.
4. **Hour four.** Add contingency.
5. **Hour five.** Add infrastructure and third party.
6. **Hour six.** Add design, content, and security.
7. **Hour seven and eight.** Review with someone who has shipped before.
