## What you actually need to know

- The recruiter engineer relationship is mostly broken because it is optimized for the transaction rather than the relationship.
- Engineers with options ignore generic outreach. Full stop. The ones who respond do so because something in the message was specific to them.
- Recruiters with long term track records build them through honesty, not volume.
- The best recruiters I know are essentially domain experts who happen to work in talent. They can hold a thirty minute technical conversation and come away with a real read on a candidate.
- On the engineer side, the habit that matters most is being explicit about what you want. Recruiters cannot advocate for you if they do not know what you are looking for.

| Recruiter type | How they work | What engineers get | What engineers risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| In house talent team | Exclusively sources for one company | Deep company knowledge, honest culture briefing | Limited to that company's open roles |
| Contingency recruiter (agency) | Paid when placement is made | Access to many companies at once | Low touch after placement, volume focus |
| Retained search firm | Paid upfront by the client company | Senior search, more thorough process | Slower timeline, more selective |
| Technical sourcer | Specialists in engineering roles | Domain knowledge, better briefings | Smaller network, higher cost to company |

## The core argument

Most engineers have a version of the same story. A recruiter found them on LinkedIn, sent an enthusiastic message about an exciting opportunity, ran them through three rounds of interviews, and then went quiet the moment the company chose someone else. No feedback. No check in three months later. No acknowledgment that they had existed.

That experience compounds. An engineer who goes through it twice stops responding to recruiter messages entirely. The recruiter who caused it moves on to the next candidate. The relationship never gets built because neither side invests in the other beyond the immediate transaction. The result is a market where the best engineers are essentially unreachable through traditional recruiting channels and the recruiter is left wondering why their response rate is two percent.

The fix is not complicated. It is just slow. A recruiter who follows up after a rejection with specific feedback, checks in three months later without an agenda, and refers the engineer to someone else in their network when the timing is wrong, builds a relationship that survives the transaction. That engineer will respond to the next message. They will refer a colleague. They will say yes to a role that might not be perfect because they trust the person who is describing it.

From the engineer's side, the fix is being explicit. Recruiters work with dozens of candidates at a time. If you want a remote role, say it clearly. If you have a compensation floor, say the number. If you are not looking right now but would be open in six months, say that. Vague signals produce generic outreach. Specific preferences produce relevant conversations.

## The habits that make the relationship work

**Habit one. Specificity in the first message.** A recruiter who references something specific from the engineer's public work, names the exact technology the role uses, and mentions the company (not "a confidential client"), is someone the engineer can take seriously. The message does not have to be long. It has to show that the recruiter did five minutes of reading.

**Habit two. Honest briefing before every interview.** Before an engineer walks into a technical interview, a good recruiter tells them everything they know. The hiring manager's communication style. The current state of the team. The known challenges. The reason the role is open. This is not a violation of confidentiality. It is doing the job correctly.

**Habit three. Feedback after rejection.** Every engineer who does not get the job should hear why. One or two sentences. Not corporate language, not vague positivity. "They wanted someone who had led a team" is actionable. "They went in a different direction" is useless. Feedback is the fastest way to build trust with an engineer who did not get the role.

**Habit four. Contact without agenda.** A recruiter who emails every three months just to check in, with no open role to discuss, is rare. That recruiter is the one who gets the call when the engineer is ready to move. The engineers worth recruiting are not on job boards. They are in the inboxes of the recruiters who stayed in touch.

### What engineers owe the recruiter

Engineers who want to be treated like partners need to behave like partners. That means returning calls when there is interest and sending a clear decline when there is not. It means not using a recruiter's briefing to get intel on a company and then applying directly. It means being honest about timelines, competing offers, and the real reason a role was not the right fit.

The engineers who do these things are the ones recruiters go out of their way for. The referrals, the early looks at unlisted roles, the honest company intel. Those things flow toward the people who make the relationship easy to maintain.

## What it actually costs

| Investment | From the recruiter | From the engineer |
|---|---|---|
| First contact | 20 minutes of research to personalize | 10 minutes to respond if the message is relevant |
| Pre interview briefing | 30 minute call | 30 minute call |
| Post rejection follow up | 10 minutes to write feedback | 5 minutes to read and respond |
| Long term relationship maintenance | Quarterly check in with no agenda | A reply when the message comes |

The table is about time, not money, because the recruiter engineer relationship is a time investment for both sides. The companies that do best in tight engineering markets are the ones whose in house recruiters have built real relationships over years, not the ones spending the most on sourcing tools.

## What to look for in a recruiter worth working with

- They name the company in the first message unless there is a specific reason not to.
- They can describe the technical stack of the role in accurate detail.
- They give feedback after rejections without being asked.
- They have engineers who will vouch for them directly, not just LinkedIn recommendations from clients.
- They ask about your preferences before submitting your resume anywhere.
- They are reachable after the placement is made.
- They push back when a client's role description is unrealistic, rather than sending candidates who will never get the offer.

## How this played out on a real project

A founder I worked with was trying to hire a senior backend engineer for a growing SaaS product. They had tried three contingency recruiting firms in six months and gotten no offers accepted. The candidates were technically reasonable but never quite right. No one had told the recruiters that the role was remote first, that the team communicated primarily through written async channels, and that the founder cared more about documentation habits than raw speed.

We sat down and wrote a two page brief: the technical scope, the communication norms, the compensation range, and the reason the previous two hires had not worked out. We shared it with one recruiter who had been referred by another founder. Three candidates in three weeks. Two final round interviews. One offer accepted. The recruiter told us afterward that the brief was the difference. They knew exactly who to look for.

## Common mistakes in the recruiter engineer relationship

1. Generic outreach at volume. One personalized message is worth more than ten template messages, measured by response rate and relationship quality.
2. Not giving feedback after rejection. This is the single fastest way to destroy a relationship that had potential.
3. Submitting to companies without explicit approval. Once a candidate is submitted, the recruiter owns that relationship with that company for some period.
4. Disappearing after placement. The commission is not the end of the relationship. It is the beginning of the reputation.
5. Using a recruiter's briefing to go around them. It is small and it spreads.
6. Not being honest about compensation expectations. Both sides waste time when the number comes out in round three.
7. Treating technical roles as interchangeable. A backend engineer and a frontend engineer are not the same role. Sending one to the other's interview is a waste of everyone's time.
8. Building a recruiter relationship only when actively looking. The best recruiter relationships are built when there is no immediate transaction on the table.

## A 60 day plan

1. **Week one.** Identify two or three technical recruiters in your market who have been referred by engineers you trust. Email them with a clear statement of what you are looking for or what you are hiring for.
2. **Week two to three.** Run the first round of conversations. Evaluate whether they brief specifically, ask smart questions, and push back on anything unrealistic.
3. **Week four.** Commit to the recruiter who earns it. Share the full brief, including compensation range and the honest reason previous searches have failed.
4. **Month two.** Check in at the midpoint of the search. Give feedback on every candidate, positive or negative. The more signal you give, the more precisely they source.
