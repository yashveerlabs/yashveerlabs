## What you need to know

- Generic outreach messages are deleted immediately by experienced engineers who receive many recruiting messages. Specific, researched outreach is the minimum bar for engagement.
- Withholding compensation ranges until late in the process wastes everyone's time and signals disrespect for the candidate's time.
- Overly long hiring processes with excessive take-home assignments lose senior candidates to shorter competing processes.
- Vague job descriptions that could apply to any engineering role signal organizational dysfunction, not a compelling opportunity.
- Honest representation of the current technical state, including challenges, attracts better candidates than overselling.

## The core argument

Senior engineers are not evaluating whether to take a job the same way junior engineers are. A junior engineer may be grateful for any credible offer. A senior engineer with five to ten years of experience and a strong portfolio is evaluating whether a role is worth leaving their current position for, whether the company appears organized and functional, and whether the recruiting process signals what it will be like to work there. Every touchpoint in the recruiting process is a signal about the company.

A recruiter who sends a copy-paste message with the wrong company name in the template is telling the senior engineer that the company does not pay attention to details. A process that asks for a 10-hour take-home assignment before a single conversation with a human is telling the candidate that their time is less valuable than the company's convenience. A hiring manager who cannot answer basic questions about the team's current technical challenges during a first interview is telling the candidate that the role is not well-defined. Senior engineers process all of these signals and make go/no-go decisions accordingly.

The recruiting approach that works for senior engineers is: specific outreach that demonstrates familiarity with the candidate's work, transparent and early communication about compensation range, a compact hiring process (under four weeks from initial contact to offer), honest representation of the current technical state including the hard parts, and respectful time constraints on any technical assessment. In my observation working both as a hiring engineer and helping companies structure their technical recruiting processes, the companies that attract strong senior engineers consistently on all of these dimensions tend to also be the companies that strong senior engineers actually want to work at.

## Common mistakes

1. **Sending the same outreach template to 200 candidates.** Mass outreach for senior engineering roles produces low response rates and occasionally embarrassing errors (wrong candidate name, wrong company name) that damage employer brand. Personalized outreach to 20 well-researched candidates produces better conversion than mass outreach to 200, because the 20 who respond are qualified and interested.

2. **Requiring a take-home assignment before any human conversation.** Asking candidates to invest 5 to 10 hours in a take-home assignment before speaking with anyone at the company signals that the company values its own time more than the candidate's. A brief introductory call first filters obviously misaligned candidates and makes the take-home feel like a mutual investment, not a qualification hurdle.

3. **Not having a clear answer to "why is this role open?"** Every senior engineer will ask why the role is open. "Backfilling a departure" is a different signal than "growing the team." If the answer is "we've had five engineers in this role in two years," the recruiter needs to understand the honest answer and be prepared to address it directly. Deflecting this question destroys trust immediately.

4. **Moving too slowly after strong interviews.** Senior engineers who have good interviews are actively considering other opportunities simultaneously. A two-week gap between the last interview and the offer letter loses candidates to companies that move faster. Establish a clear post-interview decision timeline and communicate it to candidates at the end of the interview process.

5. **Requiring references before making an offer.** Some companies ask for references as a pre-offer step, which requires candidates to alert their references (sometimes their current manager) that they are job searching before they have confirmed they want the role. Post-offer references are standard. Pre-offer references are an unnecessary friction that signals a distrustful process.

## Where to start

1. **Audit the current outreach templates and process for the patterns above.** Read the current outreach message as a senior engineer receiving it would. Does it mention anything specific about the candidate? Does it include compensation range? Does it explain why this specific role would be interesting to this specific person? If not, rewrite it.

2. **Map the full candidate timeline from first contact to offer.** Count the number of steps, estimate the total candidate time investment (including take-home), and calculate the typical calendar time from first contact to offer. Compare this to what a senior engineer who is casually looking would tolerate. Eliminate steps that do not add hiring signal.

3. **Write the job description around specific problems, not generic requirements.** Replace "strong communication skills" with the specific communication challenges of the role. Replace "modern tech stack" with the specific technologies. Replace "passionate about technology" with the specific product problems the engineer will own. Specificity attracts candidates who are interested in the actual role.
