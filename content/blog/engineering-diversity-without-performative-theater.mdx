## The core argument

Engineering diversity is one of those topics where the gap between what teams say and what they do is largest. The teams that talk about diversity loudest are often the teams whose hiring practices have not changed. The values page declares the commitment. The candidates the team interviews look like the team that already exists. The hires are the candidates that look like the team. The team does not change.

The teams that actually broaden their teams do the work quietly. They source from broader channels. They run structured interviews with diverse panels. They audit the funnel for bias. They invest in growing engineers from underrepresented groups. The work is unglamorous. The results show up in the hires.

The work matters because diverse teams build better products. The data on this is real and contested in details but consistent in direction. The teams with more diverse perspectives catch more edge cases, build for more user segments, and reach product market fit faster in markets the team did not originally see.

The investment is mostly process. Structured interviews are cheap. Diverse panels are scheduling. Broader sourcing is a few hours per role. Apprenticeship programs are real investment but pay back over years. The total cost is small. The team that does the work changes shape. The team that performs the theater stays the same.

The cost of the actions is mostly process time. A few hours per hire for the broader sourcing. A few days to build the structured interview rubric. A few thousand USD per year for conference sponsorship if applicable. An apprenticeship program is a real commitment of engineering time but pays back. The total cost is small relative to engineering payroll.

## How this played out on a real project

A client engineering team of twelve had grown homogeneously from a referral first sourcing model. The founder wanted to broaden the team but was not seeing change despite announcements and a values page.

We changed the practices. Structured interview rubric. Sourcing from three new channels including communities focused on underrepresented engineers. Diverse interview panels. Quarterly funnel audit.

The next three hires came from the new channels. The fourth came from the existing referral pool. The pattern over the next year was that roughly half of new hires came from the broader sourcing. The team's shape shifted measurably. The change came from the practices, not from the announcements.

## Common mistakes teams make

1. Announcements without action.
2. Referral only sourcing.
3. Unstructured interviews.
4. Culture fit questions that filter for the existing team.
5. No funnel audit. Bias is invisible.
6. No growth investment. Pipeline stays narrow.
7. Performative diversity reports without practice change.
8. Treating diversity as a values question rather than a practices question.

## A 90 day plan

1. **Weeks one and two.** Audit the current funnel. Identify the bias points.
2. **Weeks three and four.** Build the structured interview rubric.
3. **Weeks five and six.** Identify the new sourcing channels.
4. **Weeks seven and eight.** Train interviewers on the rubric. Establish diverse panels.
5. **Weeks nine to twelve.** Run new sourcing. Measure the funnel.
