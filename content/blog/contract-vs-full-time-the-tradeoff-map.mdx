## What you actually need to know

- Contract for defined scope work with a clear end.
- Full time for the work that needs deep context across years.
- Contract rates are 1.5 to 3 times the hourly equivalent of full time.
- Contract to hire reduces risk on both sides.
- The mistake is using either for the wrong shape of work.

| Work shape | Best fit | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Defined feature build | Contract | Scope is clear, end is defined |
| Migration project | Contract | Specialized, time bounded |
| Security audit | Contract | One time work |
| Core product engineering | Full time | Deep context, ownership |
| Founding engineer | Full time | Equity alignment |
| Specialized capability spike | Contract | Brings expertise for the duration |
| Team lead | Full time | Long horizon |
| Performance audit | Contract | One time, specialized |

## The core argument

The choice between contract and full time is shaped by the work, not by the cost. Cost comparisons that put one against the other ignore that the two are not interchangeable. The contractor is brought in for defined work with a defined end. The full time employee is brought in for the work that has no defined end and that the company expects to own for years.

The pattern that works is to use each for what fits. The contract role for the specific build. The full time role for the ownership. The teams that mix them appropriately move faster than the teams that try to do everything with either alone. The full time team carries the long horizon work. The contractors handle the specialized spikes and the time bounded projects.

The cost difference is real but smaller than it looks at first. The contract rate is 1.5 to 3 times the hourly equivalent of full time. The full time comp includes benefits, taxes, payroll overhead, and the cost of unproductive time. The total cost of a full time engineer is roughly 1.3 to 1.6 times the salary. The contract premium accounts for the contractor's self employment overhead, lack of benefits, and lack of stability. The math is closer to even than the headline rate suggests.

The deciding factor is usually fit. The work that the company wants someone to own for years should be full time. The work that has a defined end should be contract. The work that is somewhere in between can run as contract to hire. The arrangement reduces hiring risk on both sides because both parties evaluate the fit during the contract period before committing.

## The decision map

| Question | Contract | Full time |
|---|---|---|
| Does the work have a defined end? | Yes | No |
| Does the work need deep context across years? | No | Yes |
| Is the role specialized for a specific spike? | Yes | No |
| Does the role need equity alignment? | No | Yes |
| Is the work variable load that may not always exist? | Yes | No |
| Is the role part of the team identity? | No | Yes |

## How much does this cost

| Role type | Annualized cost in the US |
|---|---|
| Senior contractor at 200 USD per hour, 80 percent utilization | 333k USD |
| Senior full time at 250k salary plus benefits | 350 to 400k USD |
| Junior contractor at 100 USD per hour | 165k USD |
| Junior full time at 130k salary plus benefits | 180 to 210k USD |

The cost difference per hour is large. The total cost difference is smaller when benefits and overhead are included. The fit is usually the more important question.

## Features the contract setup must have

- A signed scope of work with deliverables.
- A defined rate and payment schedule.
- A defined timeline with milestones.
- IP transfer terms.
- Termination terms.
- Confidentiality and security expectations.
- A defined point of contact.
- A review cadence.

## How this played out on a real project

A client had hired a senior contractor for what was actually full time core engineering work. The contractor delivered within scope but the deep context the role needed was not building. The contractor was on three projects and could not give the dedicated focus the work required.

We restructured. The contractor finished the specific build they had been hired for. We hired a full time senior engineer for the core ownership work. We kept the contractor on a small retainer for occasional specialized work that came up.

The pattern worked. The full time engineer built the context the role needed. The contractor handled the spikes that the full time engineer could not absorb. The total cost was similar to the original contractor only arrangement. The outcome was much better.

## Common mistakes teams make

1. Hiring a contractor for core ownership work.
2. Hiring full time for a defined six month project.
3. Comparing rates without comparing total cost.
4. No written scope of work for contractors.
5. No IP transfer terms. Disputes follow.
6. Misclassifying full time workers as contractors. Legal risk.
7. Treating contract to hire as a way to avoid hiring decisions. It is not.
8. No clear point of contact for the contractor. Confusion follows.

## A decision framework

1. **Step one.** Define the work. Scope, deliverables, timeline.
2. **Step two.** Decide whether the work has a defined end.
3. **Step three.** Decide whether the work needs deep context across years.
4. **Step four.** Pick contract for defined end work. Full time for ownership work.
5. **Step five.** Structure the engagement with the right terms.
